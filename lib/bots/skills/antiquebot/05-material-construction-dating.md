# Construction Dating — The Physical Evidence That Cannot Lie

Marks can be faked. Style can be revived. Provenance paper can be fabricated. But the physical construction of an object was made by the tools, materials, and techniques available at the time of manufacture. Those do not go backward. When a piece claims to be 1780 but has wire nails (invented 1890) or plywood secondary (post-1905) or a Phillips head screw (post-1936), the piece is not 1780. Construction is the tiebreaker. Read it carefully.

## Nails — The Single Best Furniture Dating Tool

| Nail type | Date range | Diagnostic |
|---|---|---|
| Hand-forged rose head | Pre-1790 | Square shank, irregular head, visible hammer facets |
| Type I cut nail | 1790-1830 | Rectangular, irregular head, sheared from sheet |
| Type II cut nail | 1830-1890 | Rectangular, machined head, more uniform |
| Round wire nail | 1890+ | Round shank, machined head — the modern nail |
| Brad / finish nail | 1870+ | Thin, round, tiny head |

A piece with ONLY hand-forged rose-heads throughout is pre-1790. A piece with a mix of rose-heads in primary structure and cut nails in repairs is period with period repair. A piece with wire nails in primary structure is post-1890, full stop.

## Screws — The Second Best Tool

| Screw type | Date range | Diagnostic |
|---|---|---|
| Hand-filed / handmade | Pre-1812 | Irregular slot, off-center, irregular thread pitch, blunt tip |
| Machine-cut thread, hand-filed head | 1812-1848 | Regular thread, off-center slot, blunt tip |
| Fully machined slotted | 1848-1936 | Centered slot, sharp threads, gimlet (pointed) tip 1846+ |
| Phillips head | 1936+ | Cross recess — disqualifying for any "period" claim |
| Robertson / square drive | 1908+ (Canada mostly) | Square recess |
| Torx / star | 1967+ | Six-point star recess |

Phillips head on a "Federal period" chair = period fake or later repair. Either way, the claim collapses.

## Saw Marks — Dating Secondary Surfaces

Look at the unfinished backs, drawer bottoms, and underside:
- Straight saw marks (pit saw) — pre-1830, parallel marks slightly irregular
- Circular saw marks — 1830+ (arcing curved marks)
- Band saw marks — 1860+ (tight straight parallel, thinner than pit saw)
- No visible saw marks (planed smooth) — inconclusive, could be any period

Circular saw marks on a "1750 Queen Anne chest" = revival, period.

## Dovetails — Joinery Dating

- Hand-cut dovetails (pre-1860): variable spacing, slightly angled, knife-scored layout line visible, pins and tails often non-uniform.
- Machine-cut dovetails (Knapp joint 1870-1900): distinctive half-moon scallops on pin sides — only period is 1870-1900, diagnostic.
- Machine-cut pin-and-tail (1900+): perfectly uniform, identical spacing, straight cuts.
- Router-cut (1950+): identical uniform, often with jig marks.

Fewer, wider dovetails = earlier (pre-1820 pieces often have 3-4 dovetails on a drawer; 1860+ pieces have 6-8).

## Wood — Species And Age Diagnostics

Primary woods by period and region:
- American 18th C: walnut (pre-1750), mahogany (1750+), cherry (Connecticut valley), maple (New England tiger maple prized).
- American 19th C: mahogany, rosewood (Empire+), oak (Victorian), quartersawn oak (Arts and Crafts).
- English 18th C: walnut (Queen Anne), mahogany (post-1730), oak (country pieces).
- Continental: fruitwoods (French provincial), walnut (Italian), pine (Scandinavian painted).

Secondary woods are the tell of origin:
- American: tulip poplar (most common), white pine, chestnut (pre-1910 blight — chestnut presence dates pre-1920), yellow pine (southern).
- English: oak (invariable), pine (lower-end), deal (softwood pine).
- Plywood as secondary = post-1905 at earliest, and in furniture usually post-1920.

Old-growth lumber (pre-1900) has tight, uniform grain 20+ rings per inch. Modern plantation growth is 6-10 rings per inch. A "1780" chest built from wide-grain mahogany is wrong.

## Glass — Pontil Marks And Mold Seams

- Rough pontil mark center of base — hand-blown, pre-1860 common, pre-1900 still seen in high-end. The pontil is where the blowing iron was snapped off.
- Smooth ground pontil — hand-blown with finished base, post-1850 typical for decanters and fine glass.
- Mold seam stops at the shoulder — blown into mold then hand-finished, 1840-1905.
- Mold seam runs full to lip — machine-made, post-1903 (Owens automatic bottle machine 1904).
- Perfect uniform seam — post-1920 machine-made.

Depression glass is 1929-1940. Carnival glass is 1908-1925. Manganese glass (fluoresces green under UV) is pre-1915 (WWI cut off German manganese imports).

## Metal And Finish Dating

- Brass hardware: cast brass pre-1830, stamped brass 1830+, heavily stamped 1860+.
- Surface finish: shellac only pre-1920s, lacquer 1920+, polyurethane 1950+, water-based finishes 1980+. A "polyurethane finished 1810 chest" is refinished, not original.
- Patina: genuine patina is uneven, deeper in unwaxed recesses, lighter on handled edges. Artificial distressing is too uniform.

## Output

Populate authentication.reasoning with the specific construction evidence you observed. Pick the ONE most decisive construction fact and lead with it. If construction and marks/style disagree, say so explicitly and rule for construction. If construction evidence is not visible in photos, request photos of the specific locations (drawer bottom, nail heads, pontil mark, back of case) as top priority.
