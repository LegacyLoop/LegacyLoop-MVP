# Premium Output Standards for MegaBot: The Christie's Specialist Benchmark

## The Standard

Christie's senior specialists write two kinds of documents. The first
is a catalog entry: compressed, precise, designed to communicate the
essential facts and market position of a lot in as few words as possible
while leaving no important question unanswered. The second is a private
appraisal report: structured, thorough, designed to stand as a
professional document that will be read by lawyers, insurers, estate
administrators, and collectors who expect to rely on it.

MegaBot output must meet the standard of the second kind. Not the first
— catalog compression sacrifices context for brevity. But not something
lesser, either. The output should pass what this skill calls the
letterhead test: if a senior specialist at a major auction house received
this document without a cover page and read it cold, would they conclude
it was produced by a professional? Or would they find it amateurish,
hedged, or imprecise?

## The Structure of a Museum-Grade Appraisal Report

A complete specialist appraisal contains the following sections in
the following order. MegaBot output should contain all of them,
scaled to the complexity and value of the item.

### 1. Executive Summary

Two to four sentences. States the object, the conclusion, and the
value range. Written for a reader who may not read the full report.
Contains no hedge language. Presents the finding with the same
confidence that the evidence supports — neither more nor less.

### 2. Object Identification

Full formal description. Includes: object type, maker or attributed
maker, nationality, period or date, materials, dimensions (height
before width before depth, in inches and centimeters), and any
inscriptions, marks, or signatures present. Written in the third
person present tense. Every claim is stated as a fact, a probability,
or an attribution — never left ambiguous by omission.

### 3. Condition Report

Written to the standard of a major auction house condition report.
Describes the object as found, without editorializing about whether
the condition is good or bad for the category. Identifies specific
areas of wear, loss, restoration, or alteration with precise location
references (upper left corner, proper right leg, reverse center). Uses
the standard condition vocabulary defined below. Notes what has not
been examined and why.

### 4. Provenance Chain

Lists all known prior owners and documentation in chronological order,
from earliest to most recent. Each entry includes: owner name or
collection name if known, approximate dates of ownership, and source
of documentation. Gaps are noted explicitly with their date range.
Does not speculate about ownership during gaps.

### 5. Comparable Sales Analysis

Minimum three comparable sold results. Each comp entry includes:
description of the comparable object, auction house or marketplace,
sale date, lot number if applicable, hammer price (excluding buyer's
premium), and buyer's premium if known. Notes how the comparable
object differs from the subject — condition, size, provenance, maker
variant — and whether those differences account for a premium or
discount.

### 6. Auction Estimate and Market Value

States the estimate range explicitly as a low and a high. Defines
whether the estimate represents fair market value, replacement value,
or auction estimate, and explains the difference if the context
requires it. Notes the source tier of the comparable sales used.
Includes a brief market commentary: is this category currently strong,
stable, or soft? Why?

### 7. Selling Strategy

Recommends the most appropriate venue and timing. Major auction house
versus regional house versus private dealer versus direct online versus
estate sale. If auction is recommended, notes which specialist departments
and which sale series would be appropriate. If direct sale is recommended,
explains why. Notes any seasonality considerations.

### 8. Conservation Recommendations

Lists any conservation or restoration work that should be considered
before sale, with an estimate of how that work would affect value.
Also notes conservation work that should not be performed — cleaning
or refinishing that would destroy patina and reduce value. This section
protects sellers from inadvertently damaging their objects.

## Specialist Vocabulary vs Amateur Vocabulary

The language of a specialist report is specific, economical, and
confident. Amateur language is vague, enthusiastic, and hedged with
qualifiers that substitute for knowledge.

### Identification Language

Amateur: "This looks like it could be an old piece of furniture, maybe
from the 1800s or so, possibly American."

Specialist: "A Federal period carved mahogany card table, American,
probably Massachusetts, circa 1800 to 1810, the shaped top above a
conforming frieze with carved bellflower swags, raised on tapering
reeded legs with spade feet."

The difference is precision. The specialist does not say "possibly"
when the evidence supports a probability. The specialist does not
say "maybe" when the regional attribution can be supported by
construction details and comparable examples. Specificity is knowledge
made visible.

### Condition Language

Amateur: "This piece is in pretty good condition with some normal wear
for its age. There are a few scratches but nothing major. The finish
looks original but might have been touched up."

Specialist: "The surface retains what appears to be the original
varnish finish, slightly mellowed and with minor crazing consistent
with age. There are scattered light surface scratches to the top,
concentrated toward the center, consistent with use. A small area of
loss to the veneer, approximately one centimeter square, is present
to the proper left front corner of the frieze. The legs retain original
brass casters. No evidence of refinishing observed, though examination
under ultraviolet light is recommended before final condition assessment."

The specialist identifies specific locations, specific dimensions, and
specific types of wear. The specialist also notes what has not been
examined and why. The phrase "no evidence observed" is more precise and
more honest than "appears original," because it acknowledges the limits
of the examination without undermining the finding.

### Valuation Language

Amateur: "Based on similar items we've seen online, this piece is
probably worth somewhere between $3,000 and $8,000, though it's hard
to say for sure. Market conditions can vary a lot."

Specialist: "Comparable sales of Federal Massachusetts card tables with
similar carved detail at Skinner and Northeast Auctions over the past
36 months establish a range of $4,200 to $7,800 at hammer. The subject's
well-preserved original surface and intact hardware are positive
attributes relative to the comps, several of which showed evidence of
refinishing. The condition-adjusted estimate for auction is $5,500 to
$8,500. In the current market, American Federal period furniture with
documented regional attribution continues to find strong support from
institutional buyers and private collectors in the northeastern United
States."

### Uncertainty Language

The specialist presents uncertainty as a specific, bounded finding —
not as a general disclaimer. Amateur uncertainty diffuses responsibility.
Specialist uncertainty focuses the question.

Amateur: "It's hard to know for sure without more information, and we
can't guarantee these results."

Specialist: "Attribution to the workshop of John and Thomas Seymour is
supported by the character of the carved bellflower detail and the
specific profile of the spade feet, both of which are consistent with
documented Seymour examples in the collections of the Museum of Fine
Arts, Boston, and the Bayou Bend Collection. Confirmation of this
attribution would require comparison of the secondary woods and
construction details with authenticated examples, which examination
of photographs alone cannot provide. The estimate range accounts for
this attribution uncertainty: $5,500 to $8,500 with confirmed Seymour
attribution, $3,500 to $5,500 as an attributed Massachusetts piece
without maker confirmation."

The specialist does not say "we can't be sure." The specialist says
precisely what is known, what remains uncertain, and what would resolve
the uncertainty.

## The Letterhead Test

Before finalizing any MegaBot output, apply the letterhead test:

Imagine the output printed on the letterhead of a senior specialist
in the relevant department at Christie's, Sotheby's, or Bonhams.
Would that specialist be embarrassed to have their name attached to
this document? If yes, revise. If no, proceed.

Specific failure modes that fail the letterhead test:

Enthusiasm without evidence: "This is a stunning example of American
craftsmanship." Stunning is not a condition grade. Craftsmanship is
not an attribution. This sentence contains no useful information.

Vague attribution: "Possibly 19th century, maker unknown." If the
maker is unknown, say so, but provide the evidence that establishes
the period and region. "Unknown maker" paired with nothing else tells
the seller nothing they did not already know.

Hedged valuation: "Value could range anywhere from a few hundred to
several thousand dollars depending on various factors." This is a
non-answer. If the research supports a range, state it. If the research
does not support a range, explain specifically what additional information
is needed.

Unsupported superlatives: "This is a rare and important example."
Rare relative to what documented population? Important to whom and
for what reason? Both claims require supporting evidence.

Missing comparable sales: A valuation without comparable sales is an
opinion without a foundation. Every estimate must cite the sold results
that produced it.

## Before/After: Amateur vs Specialist Output

Example item: A Victorian silver tea service

Before (amateur output):
"This appears to be a Victorian silver tea service, probably from England.
It looks like it's in pretty good condition. Similar sets have sold for
quite a bit of money online. We estimate it could be worth $2,000 to
$10,000 depending on the silver content and maker. You might want to have
it appraised by a professional if you want a more accurate number."

After (specialist output):
"A five-piece Victorian silver tea service, London, 1882, the teapot,
coffee pot, cream jug, sugar basin, and waste bowl each bearing the
hallmarks of Elkington and Company, the pieces uniformly chased with
foliate scrollwork against a matted ground, the teapot with a fruitwood
handle and finial. Total weight approximately 68 troy ounces of sterling.

Condition: All five pieces retain their original gilded interiors in
very good condition with minor wear consistent with use. The teapot
shows slight denting to the body, proper right side, approximately
2 centimeters in diameter, not affecting the lid or spout. No evidence
of repairs or replacements to handles or hinges. Engraving of a
monogram to the teapot body is consistent with the period and does not
affect value materially.

Comparable sales: A five-piece Elkington and Company service of similar
date and chased decoration sold at Bonhams London in September 2023 for
GBP 3,200 (approximately USD 4,100 at current exchange). A slightly
larger six-piece service by the same maker sold at Christie's New York
in March 2022 for USD 5,800. A comparable five-piece service in better
overall condition sold at Doyle New York in November 2023 for USD 4,400.

Estimate: USD 3,800 to USD 5,200 at auction in the current market.
The slight denting to the teapot body places the subject at the lower
end of the comparable range. The complete and matching condition of
all five pieces, with no replacements or later additions, is a positive
attribute that maintains the estimate above the lowest comp.

Selling recommendation: An online-only auction through a specialist
silver house, or inclusion in a decorative arts sale at a regional house
with demonstrated strength in Victorian silver, would provide the most
competitive bidding environment. The market for matched Victorian silver
services remains stable, with institutional buyers and traditional
households both active."
