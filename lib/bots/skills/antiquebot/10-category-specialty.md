# Category Specialty — The Four Major Antique Categories

AntiqueBot handles items across dozens of categories, but four dominate both the estate market and the value concentration: silver, pottery and porcelain, furniture, and jewelry. This pack gives the specialist vocabulary, sub-market structure, and category-specific diagnostics for each. Load it alongside Pack 02 (maker marks) and Pack 05 (construction dating) for the full analytical toolkit.

## SECTION 1 — SILVER

### Forms You Must Recognize
Tea and coffee services: teapot, coffee pot, sugar bowl, cream jug, waste bowl. Georgian services are rare and valuable as complete sets; Victorian tea sets are common and less dear. Flatware services: fiddle, King's, Queen's, rat-tail, Hanoverian patterns. Hollowware: tureens, meat domes, entree dishes, cake baskets, candlesticks. Specialty: monteiths, punch bowls, inkstands, snuff boxes, vinaigrettes, caddy spoons, mote spoons.

### The Three Silver Sub-Markets
1. **Sterling flatware** — sold by weight plus maker premium. A Gorham "Buttercup" teaspoon is worth maker premium AND melt value combined.
2. **Hollowware** — sold by form, maker, and period. Weight is secondary. A Paul Revere mug is priced on historical importance, not weight.
3. **Presentation silver** — engraved pieces with named recipients. The inscription ADDS value when the recipient is notable and SUBTRACTS when anonymous.

### Silver-Specific Red Flags
- Electroplate stamped "EPNS" or "EP" or "A1" — not sterling, worth a fraction
- "Silver on Copper" or "Sheffield plate" — the predecessor to electroplate, pre-1840, has its own market but not sterling
- "German silver" or "nickel silver" — contains no silver, worthless in silver market
- "800 silver" — Continental, .800 fineness, not sterling .925
- Weighted bases on candlesticks and vases — filled with pitch or cement, silver content is only the shell

### Silver Value Drivers
Maker (Tiffany, Gorham, Kirk, Paul Revere) > period (Georgian beats Victorian) > form (rare forms beat common) > weight (for flatware) > condition (no monogram erasure) > provenance.

## SECTION 2 — POTTERY AND PORCELAIN

### Hard Paste vs Soft Paste vs Stoneware vs Earthenware
- **Hard paste porcelain** — true porcelain, translucent, rings when tapped. Meissen invented it in Europe 1710. Asian makers had it centuries earlier.
- **Soft paste porcelain** — English 18th C alternative, warmer color, softer, less translucent. Bow, Chelsea, Derby early, Worcester early.
- **Stoneware** — vitrified clay, not translucent, dense. Wedgwood Jasperware, salt-glaze stoneware, English brown stoneware.
- **Earthenware** — porous clay body, painted or glazed surface. Delft, creamware, pearlware, majolica, ironstone.

You must know which you are looking at before you attribute. A "Meissen-style" creamware is not Meissen at any price.

### American Art Pottery Hierarchy
Rookwood (Cincinnati, 1880-1967, largest and best documented) > Newcomb (New Orleans women's college pottery, 1895-1940, small production, prized) > Grueby (Boston, matte green, short run, prized) > Teco (Chicago, architectural) > Van Briggle (Colorado) > Weller, Roseville, Owens (mass market). Artist-signed pieces from any of these are premium; jiggered or machine-made are baseline.

### Porcelain Value Drivers
Period (early Meissen beats late) > maker > artist (for signed pieces) > condition (crack-free is everything) > form rarity > decoration quality.

### The "No Chips, No Cracks, No Repairs" Rule
Porcelain buyers enforce this rule more strictly than any other category. A 10 percent chip can take 40 percent off. Always disclose. Always photograph the rim from multiple angles. Always note restoration.

## SECTION 3 — FURNITURE

### The American Regional Schools
- **Newport, RI** — Goddard and Townsend families, block-front case pieces, shell carving, among the most valuable American antiques ever.
- **Philadelphia** — Savery, Randolph, Affleck, Chippendale highboys and chairs with pierced splats and acanthus knee carving. Peak American furniture.
- **New York** — Phyfe (Federal period), more European in feel.
- **Boston and Salem** — Seymour, McIntire carving, Federal bow-fronts, Seymour ladies' tambour desks.
- **Connecticut Valley** — cherry country furniture, scrolled pediment highboys.
- **Rural Pennsylvania** — Schrank, painted Germanic furniture, dower chests.

Naming the region correctly signals you are a specialist. Naming it wrong marks you as an amateur.

### Form Hierarchy By Value
In period American Chippendale, the value order roughly runs: highboy > block-front chest > tea table > lowboy > side chair > tavern table > simple country pieces. A signed Newport block-front chest at Christie's can exceed $500,000; a Connecticut country tavern table is $2,000-$5,000.

### The Original Surface Premium
Stated in Pack 08 and worth repeating: original surface is the single biggest condition premium on period American furniture. A period Chippendale chest with original finish is worth 2-3x the same chest refinished, even if the refinish is "nicely done." Sellers refinishing grandma's chest to "make it look new" have cost themselves five figures in documented cases.

## SECTION 4 — JEWELRY

### Period Divisions
- **Georgian** 1714-1837 — closed backs, foil-backed stones, hand-made, rare
- **Victorian** 1837-1901 — sub-divided into Romantic (1837-1860), Grand (1860-1885), Aesthetic (1885-1901). Jet mourning jewelry is Victorian.
- **Art Nouveau** 1895-1910 — natural forms, enamel, plique-à-jour
- **Edwardian / Belle Epoque** 1901-1915 — platinum, diamonds, lace-like filigree
- **Art Deco** 1915-1935 — geometric, platinum, calibre-cut gems
- **Retro** 1935-1950 — large, gold, bold forms
- **Mid-century** 1950-1970 — Van Cleef, Cartier, Tiffany period

### Value Drivers
Signed pieces (Cartier, Van Cleef and Arpels, Tiffany, Boucheron, Bulgari) > unsigned period > unsigned modern. A signed Art Deco bracelet can be 5-20x the same bracelet unsigned.

### Metal And Stone Content
Period gold is marked (or unmarked but testable). European gold uses karat marks 9/14/18/22 or fineness 375/585/750/916. Platinum marked PLAT or PT950. Sterling marked 925. Diamond weights estimated conservatively; color and clarity affect value more than size on antique diamonds.
