# Period Vs Style — The Most-Confused Distinction In Antiques

The hardest idea to communicate to estate sellers is this: "Chippendale" can mean four different things.

1. Period Chippendale — furniture made in the Chippendale style BETWEEN approximately 1755 and 1790.
2. Chippendale Revival — furniture made in the style DURING a revival period (Centennial revival 1876, Colonial Revival 1890-1940).
3. In-the-manner-of — furniture made later that visually references the style but does not attempt to deceive.
4. Reproduction — furniture made to imitate a period piece, including deliberate aging.

Only the first is "period" and commands the period price. The rest have their own smaller markets. A seller saying "it is Chippendale" almost always means #2 or #3. Your job is to tell which one without insulting them.

## The American Furniture Style Calendar

Learn this calendar cold. Every period has dominant construction features that back up the style vocabulary.

| Style | American dates | Dominant diagnostic |
|---|---|---|
| William and Mary | 1700-1725 | Trumpet-turned legs, stretchers, bun feet |
| Queen Anne | 1725-1755 | Cabriole legs, pad/trifid feet, no carving, pierced splat backs |
| Chippendale | 1755-1790 | Cabriole legs with ball-and-claw feet, pierced Gothic/Chinese splats, carved knees |
| Federal / Hepplewhite | 1790-1815 | Tapered square legs, inlay, shield backs, light proportions |
| Sheraton | 1790-1820 | Reeded turned legs, inlay, delicate |
| American Empire | 1815-1840 | Heavy, scroll feet, flame mahogany veneer, columns |
| Victorian | 1840-1890 | Rococo, Renaissance, Eastlake sub-styles; darker woods; machine elements appear |
| Arts and Crafts | 1895-1920 | Quartersawn oak, exposed joinery, Stickley/Roycroft |
| Art Nouveau | 1895-1910 | Curvilinear, botanical motifs, French |
| Art Deco | 1920-1940 | Geometric, exotic veneers, chrome, bakelite |
| Mid-Century Modern | 1945-1970 | Teak, walnut, tapered legs, Danish/Scandinavian |

## The English Style Calendar (Similar But Offset)

English style leads American by 10-20 years. Key differences:
- Jacobean / Carolean (1603-1688) has no American parallel.
- William and Mary English dates are 1689-1702, American is 1700-1725.
- Queen Anne English 1702-1714 (walnut age), American 1725-1755 (mahogany starting).
- Chippendale English 1750-1779 (book published 1754), American 1755-1790.
- George III / Hepplewhite / Sheraton overlap in English usage 1760-1810.
- Regency 1800-1830, no exact American parallel (American Empire is cousin).

A seller saying "Chippendale" with a piece that is clearly walnut and has Queen Anne pad feet has mixed periods — correct them politely: "The legs read Queen Anne rather than Chippendale. This piece is likely c. 1740 if period."

## The Revival Problem

American Colonial Revival (1890-1940) produced enormous quantities of well-made furniture in period style. Colonial Revival Chippendale chests are GOOD furniture — often better construction than some period pieces — but they are not worth period-piece money. The price delta is typically 5 to 20 times.

How to tell revival from period:
- Screws. Period (pre-1815): no screws, wooden pegs, rose-head nails. 1815-1880: cut-nail screws with slotted heads and off-center slot. 1880+: fully machine-made screws, centered slot, sharp threads.
- Dovetails. Period: hand-cut, variable spacing, slightly angled, knife-scored layout line visible. Revival: machine-cut, perfectly even, uniform angles.
- Saw marks. Period: straight saw marks (pit saw) pre-1830, circular saw marks 1830+, band saw 1860+. Circular saw marks on a "1750 Queen Anne chest" = revival.
- Secondary woods. Period American: tulip poplar, pine, chestnut (pre-blight 1910). Revival: gum, poplar, plywood. Plywood inside = post-1905 at earliest.
- Patina. Period: uneven, deeper in wear zones, subtle. Revival: applied patina, too even, often chemically darkened.
- Lumber. Period mahogany is old-growth, tight-grained, heavy. Revival mahogany is plantation-grown, wider grain, lighter. 18th-century oak on English pieces is often tight-grained and very dense.

## Silver Style Periods

- Queen Anne silver — plain, simple forms, 1702-1720.
- Rococo — repoussé, asymmetrical, 1730-1770.
- Neoclassical / Adam — urn shapes, beaded borders, 1770-1810.
- Regency silver — heavy, gadrooned, 1810-1830.
- Victorian — ornate, chased, 1840-1890.
- Arts and Crafts silver — hammered surface, simple forms, 1895-1915 (Kalo, Stone).
- Art Nouveau silver — Tiffany Magnolia, Gorham Martelé, 1895-1910.
- Art Deco silver — Puiforcat, Jensen, 1920-1940.

## Ceramics Style Periods

- Early Meissen 1710-1756, Marcolini period 1774-1814, 19th C Meissen 1814-1900, 20th C 1900+.
- Chinese export Kangxi 1662-1722, Qianlong 1736-1795, 19th C export.
- English creamware 1760+, pearlware 1780+, whiteware 1820+, ironstone 1813+.
- American art pottery: Rookwood 1880-1967, Newcomb 1895-1940, Grueby 1894-1909, Teco 1886-1923, Van Briggle 1899-present.

## When To Say "Period" And When To Hedge

Say "period" only when construction, style, AND at least one objective dating feature (mark, documented provenance, characteristic wear pattern) all agree. Otherwise say:
- "Style of Chippendale, American, likely Colonial Revival c. 1910-1930."
- "In the Queen Anne taste, English, 20th century."
- "Chippendale period possible, construction suggestive, pending drawer-bottom and foot examination."

## Output

Populate identification.era with the specific decade or twenty-year window. Populate identification.style with the correct style label. In authentication.reasoning, cite which construction features you used to support or refute the period claim. If you said "period," list at least three independent diagnostics that all agreed. If you said "revival" or "style of," list the single disqualifying feature.
