# YouTube Shorts Standards

## Why Shorts Is Different from Every Other Platform

YouTube Shorts occupies a unique position in the short-form video ecosystem because it sits on top of the world's second-largest search engine. While TikTok and Instagram Reels are discovery platforms — they push content to users who did not ask for it — YouTube is fundamentally a search platform that now has a short-form discovery layer on top.

This distinction drives every decision VideoBot makes for Shorts scripts. The intent signal is different: a user who finds a Shorts video through the Shorts shelf may be in passive scroll mode, but a user who finds it through search has expressed an explicit interest. Writing for both modes simultaneously is the Shorts challenge.

VideoBot targets 45-55 seconds for Shorts content. YouTube allows up to 60 seconds for Shorts classification. Unlike TikTok's strict 30-second discipline, Shorts benefits from its extra length budget because the platform rewards knowledge transfer — delivering genuine information value — which requires slightly more time than a pure entertainment hook-and-reveal.

## YouTube SEO for Short Content

The title is the single most important SEO element of a Shorts video. YouTube's search algorithm treats Shorts titles identically to long-form video titles in terms of keyword indexing and ranking signals.

The critical discipline: Shorts titles must contain searchable keywords, not marketing language or viral hooks.

Wrong: "OMG Look At What I Found At This Estate Sale"
Wrong: "You Won't Believe The Value Of This Item"
Right: "1924 Singer Featherweight Sewing Machine Estate Find — Worth $400"
Right: "Tiffany Studio Bronze Lamp Base Identification and Value"
Right: "1967 Shelby GT500 Numbers Matching Barn Find — 55 Year Sleep"

The correct title formula: [Item Name/Model] + [Context] + [Value/Hook Signal]

This formula serves two audiences simultaneously: search users who know what they are looking for ("Singer Featherweight sewing machine value") and Shorts shelf users who are drawn by the hook signal ("Estate Find," "Barn Find," "55 Year Sleep") without needing to have searched for the item.

Description should include long-tail keyword phrases — the specific search terms a collector or researcher would type when looking for information about this category of item. For a Civil War era compass: "antique brass compass value," "Civil War military equipment identification," "surveying compass estate sale."

The hashtag #Shorts is mandatory for YouTube to classify the video in the Shorts feed. Include 2-3 additional category hashtags.

## The Informational Hook

The TikTok hook leads with surprise or curiosity. The Shorts hook leads with the value proposition — what knowledge will the viewer gain by watching this video?

The informational hook formula: State the payoff up front.

"This $40 yard sale find is worth $4,000 — and I will show you exactly how to tell the difference."
"I found a numbers-matching 1969 Camaro in a barn. Here is what that actually means for value."
"This maker's mark is worth memorizing. It appears on undervalued pieces at every estate sale."
"Three things to check before you walk past an old tool chest at a yard sale."

The informational hook works on Shorts for two reasons: it captures search intent (the viewer who searched for this topic gets immediate confirmation they are in the right place) and it creates a knowledge gap (the Shorts shelf viewer who was not searching now wants the information that was promised).

Notice the structural difference from TikTok: the Shorts informational hook often promises a framework or a lesson, not just a reveal. "Here is what that actually means for value" and "I will show you exactly how to tell the difference" signal knowledge transfer, which is YouTube's core value proposition to its audience.

## Expert Knowledge Transfer Tone

TikTok rewards "person reacting to discovery." Shorts rewards "expert sharing knowledge." These are fundamentally different performance modes and require different script writing.

The Shorts narrator is a knowledgeable guide — someone who has seen hundreds or thousands of items like this one and can contextualize what they are seeing for the viewer. The tone is measured, informative, and authoritative without being condescending.

Compare:

TikTok version: "Wait — I almost walked past this. The price tag said $12. Twelve dollars. I grabbed it immediately because I recognized what this was and I could not believe nobody else had."

Shorts version: "This is a Gorham sterling silver tea service, circa 1890. The lion passant on the base confirms sterling content, and the date letter places it in the early Arts and Crafts period. Estate sales routinely underprice sterling pieces because sellers assess by weight, not by maker. Current auction value for a matched set in this condition: $800 to $1,400."

Both scripts describe the same find. The TikTok version is emotionally driven; the Shorts version is informationally driven. Both are effective for their respective platforms; neither would perform as well on the other platform.

## The Repeat Viewing Signal

YouTube's algorithm for Shorts shelf recommendation uses watch time and repeat viewing as primary ranking signals. Videos that are rewatched rank significantly higher than videos that are watched once and swiped past, even if total view counts are similar.

The structural technique for generating rewatches: include specific facts — numbers, dates, identification marks, valuation details — that the viewer will want to rewatch to capture accurately. A viewer who watches to take notes on the maker's mark or the price range will rewatch the relevant section, generating algorithmic signal.

VideoBot Shorts scripts deliberately include at least two pieces of specific, memorable information that a motivated viewer would rewatch to capture: a specific date or era, a valuation range, a physical identification marker, or a search term to look up.

This also serves a secondary purpose: repeat viewing generates the data that trains YouTube's recommendation algorithm to identify this video as high-quality content for the established viewer and for viewers with similar search and watch history.

## The 60-Second Structural Budget

Shorts content can run to 60 seconds — longer than TikTok or Reels — but this budget should be used deliberately, not permissively. The structure:

HOOK (0-5 seconds): Informational value proposition. What will the viewer know at the end of this video that they do not know now?

IDENTIFICATION (5-20 seconds): What is the item? Category, maker, era, construction details, and identifying features. This section is the most YouTube-native section of the script — it is pure knowledge transfer.

CONDITION AND RARITY (20-35 seconds): What is the condition of this specific example? What makes it rare or common within its category? What distinguishes a high-value example from a low-value example?

VALUATION (35-50 seconds): What is it worth, and why? Reference comparable sales if available. Give a price range with context. Explain what drives the value up or down.

CTA (50-60 seconds): Subscribe for more finds in this category, comment with questions, or link in description for purchase or inquiry. The Shorts CTA should reinforce the educational brand: "Subscribe to learn how to spot undervalued antiques."

## Shorts Shelf vs Search: The Dual Audience

Shorts distribution comes from two distinct sources: the Shorts shelf (algorithm-driven, similar to TikTok/Reels) and search results (intent-driven, unique to YouTube). A well-optimized Shorts video performs in both channels simultaneously.

Shelf audience characteristics: passive browser, no specific intent, in entertainment mode, needs to be hooked immediately.

Search audience characteristics: active researcher, specific intent (finding information about a category or item), in learning mode, will tolerate a slower hook if the content delivers on the search promise.

VideoBot balances both audiences by opening with an informational hook (satisfies the search audience) that is also visually and verbally compelling (hooks the shelf audience). The hook "This $40 yard sale find is worth $4,000" works for both: the search audience recognizes the category and the shelf audience is stopped by the value mismatch.

## What Makes a Short Get Recommended vs Buried

The Shorts that get buried share common failure modes: titles that are not keyword-rich, hooks that do not deliver on their promise, content that runs long without sustaining value delivery, missing #Shorts hashtag, and CTAs that do not align with what the viewer came to the video for.

The Shorts that get recommended share common success patterns: searchable keyword-rich titles, informational hooks that promise and deliver specific knowledge, at least two rewatchable specific facts, and a subscribe CTA that reinforces an educational brand identity.

For estate and antique content specifically, the category has a deeply engaged search audience — collectors, researchers, appraisers, and hobbyists actively search for identification and valuation information. A Shorts library built around this content category accumulates algorithmic authority over time, with each video reinforcing the recommendation signal for all others in the catalog.
