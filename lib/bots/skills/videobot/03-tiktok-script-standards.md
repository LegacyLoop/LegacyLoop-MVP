# TikTok Script Standards

## The 30-Second Constraint

TikTok's algorithm distributes content based primarily on completion rate — the percentage of viewers who watch a video from beginning to end, or who rewatch it. A 30-second video that 70 percent of viewers complete outperforms a 60-second video that 40 percent complete by every algorithmic measure.

VideoBot enforces a strict 30-second maximum for all TikTok scripts. At standard narration pace (approximately 125-140 words per minute), this translates to a word budget of 65 to 70 words. Every word in the script must earn its place.

This constraint is not a limitation — it is a discipline. It forces the writer to identify what is actually most compelling about the item and eliminate everything else. The compression required to tell a compelling story in 30 seconds is what makes TikTok content addictive.

## The HOOK-REVEAL-VALUE-CTA Structure

Every TikTok script follows this four-beat structure with precise timing:

HOOK (0-3 seconds): The scroll-stopper. One to two short sentences maximum. Must work as a spoken line and as an on-screen text overlay simultaneously. The hook should be deliverable in under four seconds at speaking pace.

REVEAL (3-8 seconds): The item is shown and its core identity established. What is it? Where did it come from? What makes it different from what the viewer expected? This is where the hook's promise begins to be fulfilled.

VALUE (8-20 seconds): The substantive middle. Condition, history, pricing context, rarity, or story — whichever is most compelling for this specific item. This is the longest beat and where most of the word budget is spent. The value section must move quickly; there is no room for padding.

CTA (20-30 seconds): The close. Where can they find it? What should they do next? Comments, likes, follows, DMs, or links in bio. The CTA must match the emotional tone established by the hook — a sentimental story closes with a warm CTA; a shocking value reveal closes with an urgent CTA.

## Gen Z Language Calibration

TikTok's primary demographic is 18-34, with a center of gravity in the 20-26 range. This audience was raised on internet culture and has developed acute sensitivity to inauthenticity. Corporate language, over-produced enthusiasm, and careful hedging all register as red flags.

The correct tone is: authentic over polished. Slightly unhinged enthusiasm over measured corporate restraint. "No way" energy over "quite remarkable" energy.

What this means in practice:

Preferred: "I cannot believe what I paid for this." / "This thing is absolutely unreal." / "Nobody wanted this. That was wild to me." / "The price I found shocked me."

Avoid: "This item presents an exceptional investment opportunity." / "We are pleased to offer this remarkable piece." / "Condition is excellent with minor wear consistent with age."

The vocabulary of TikTok antique and thrift content has its own register: "hidden gem," "thrift flip," "estate find," "my jaw dropped," "the appraiser said WHAT," "I almost walked past this." These are authentic cultural signals, not marketing language.

At the same time, TikTok rewards specificity. Vague enthusiasm underperforms specific facts. "This is really cool" is weak. "This sold at Christie's for $8,400 in 2019" is strong.

## Pattern Interrupt in the First Frame

The first frame of a TikTok video — what is visible in the first 0.5 seconds before the audio begins — determines whether a viewer pauses on the video or swipes past it. This is separate from the verbal hook.

VideoBot scripts should include a visual direction for the first frame that describes the pattern interrupt. Options:

- Item shown from an unexpected angle (bottom, back, inside drawer)
- The most visually striking feature of the item in extreme close-up
- Text overlay with the most surprising single fact about the item
- The before-and-after frame (item as found vs. item cleaned up)
- A hand holding the item with an expression of visible surprise or reverence

The visual pattern interrupt and the verbal hook work as a two-channel attention capture system. Together they are more powerful than either alone.

## Trending Audio Alignment

TikTok audio trends change weekly. VideoBot does not hardcode specific audio recommendations because they expire. Instead, scripts are written to function at three levels:

Level one: The script works as narration with any neutral background music. This is the baseline requirement.

Level two: The script's pacing and emotional beats align with trending audio categories (dramatic reveal sounds, satisfying discovery sounds, nostalgic or sentimental instrumental tracks). Scripts should note the emotional category of audio that would enhance the content.

Level three: The script's structure is designed to work with duet and stitch formats, allowing other creators to react, respond, or add commentary — which multiplies distribution.

## Algorithm Optimization

Completion rate is the primary ranking signal on TikTok. Write to be rewatched. The structural technique for rewatch optimization: end the video with something that makes the viewer want to go back to the beginning. A detail revealed at the end that changes how the hook lands. A price reveal that makes the opening frame more interesting in retrospect.

Shares and saves outweigh likes on TikTok's algorithm. A video that 200 people save is more algorithmically valuable than a video that 2,000 people like. Scripts should be written to be "save-worthy" — content the viewer wants to reference later or share with a specific person.

The save-worthy test: ask whether a viewer would want to bookmark this for future reference (instructional content, pricing data, identification tips) or share it with a specific person they know ("my mom would love this," "I have to show this to my friend who collects these").

Comments also drive distribution. Scripts should include a comment-bait element: a question, a challenge, a surprising claim that viewers want to respond to or dispute.

## Duet and Stitch Bait

Scripts that generate duets and stitches from other creators receive a significant algorithmic boost. Duet and stitch bait is any element of the script that invites a natural response format.

Effective duet/stitch bait for antique and estate content:

- Pose a valuation question that an expert viewer would want to answer: "I think this is worth around $800. Antique dealers — am I wrong?"
- Make a claim that invites verification: "I was told this is from the 1890s. What do you think?"
- Display an item that other creators might own: "If you have one of these, check the bottom."
- Issue a direct challenge to the audience: "Try to guess what this is before the reveal."

## TikTok CTA Hierarchy

The most effective CTAs for antique and estate items on TikTok, ranked by conversion rate:

1. "Comment INTERESTED and I will DM you details." (Drives comments + creates qualified leads)
2. "Follow for more finds like this." (List building)
3. "Link in bio to the listing." (Direct to purchase — lower click rate but highest purchase intent)
4. "Save this if you know someone who collects [category]." (Drives saves + word of mouth)
5. "What would you pay for this? Drop your guess in the comments." (Drives comments + engagement)

Every TikTok script must end with exactly one CTA. Multiple CTAs dilute action.

## What Gets Swiped Past

VideoBot avoids the following patterns, which consistently underperform on TikTok:

- Opening with "Hi everyone" or any greeting
- Starting with the seller's name or channel name
- Lengthy scene-setting before the hook
- Apology language ("Sorry for the shaky camera")
- Overly formal or overly hedged language
- Listing features instead of telling a story
- CTA that requires too much cognitive effort ("Visit our website and filter by category")
- Video that ends without a CTA
- Running longer than 30 seconds without a compelling reason that serves completion rate

The question to ask of every line in a TikTok script: if this line were removed, would the video be weaker? If the answer is no, remove it.
