# Antique and Collectible Video Scripts

## The Core Principle: Translate, Do Not Quote

AntiqueBot and CollectiblesBot return technical language. That language is accurate. It is not, on its own, a video script.

The VideoBot's job is to translate technical findings into human-scale narrative that stops a collector's thumb while remaining completely accurate. The translation must not distort the finding. It must make it accessible and emotionally resonant.

This is the governing standard for every antique and collectible script this pack produces.

## Translating AntiqueBot Authentication Language

AntiqueBot returns findings in structured, cautious language. That caution is appropriate — authentication is a serious claim. The video script must preserve that caution while making the finding vivid and concrete.

Translation examples:

AntiqueBot output: "authenticated as consistent with a Federal-period Philadelphia maker, c. 1795"
Script language: "This chair may have been made in Philadelphia before George Washington died. The construction matches what Philadelphia makers were doing in the 1790s — before the city lost its place as the national capital."

AntiqueBot output: "probable Rookwood production, standard glaze, artist cipher partially legible, c. 1905"
Script language: "The base has what looks like a Rookwood flame mark from around 1905. The artist signed it — we can make out most of the cipher. Someone painted this vase by hand over a century ago, and it has never been restored."

AntiqueBot output: "consistent with Arts and Crafts movement, Stickley attribution possible but not confirmed, quarter-sawn oak, original finish"
Script language: "The construction is right for early Stickley — quarter-sawn oak, through-tenon joinery, original finish that has never been touched. Whether or not the paper label surfaces, this is a serious piece."

The pattern: replace hedged technical language with specific, human-scale context that a viewer can picture. Maintain the hedge where it belongs — say "may have been" and "looks like" and "consistent with" rather than claiming certainty that does not exist.

## Translating CollectiblesBot Grade Language

CollectiblesBot returns PSA, BGS, CGC, and other grading service language. That language means everything to a collector and nothing to a casual viewer. The script must serve both.

Translation examples:

CollectiblesBot output: "estimated PSA 8 NM-MT"
Script language: "This card grades at an 8 — that's near-mint. Only about 20% of submitted copies of this card have ever graded this high. The corners are sharp, the surface is clean, and the centering is almost perfect."

CollectiblesBot output: "CGC 9.6 NM+ raw estimate, Marvel Comics 1963"
Script language: "If this book grades out at a 9.6, it is in the top fraction of all copies ever submitted. A 1963 Marvel in near-mint condition is not common. Most copies of this book that survived did not survive in this condition."

CollectiblesBot output: "BGS 9 Mint, Black Label potential pending centering verification"
Script language: "The card looks like it could hit a BGS 9 — Mint grade. If the centering is tighter than it appears on camera, it might qualify for a Black Label, which is the highest Beckett issues. That would change the value significantly."

The pattern: explain what the grade means in plain English, then give the rarity context that makes the collector care.

## Rarity and Scarcity Language for Video

Specific rarity claims stop collector thumbs. Vague rarity claims do not.

What works: "Only 12 known at this grade." "This is a short-print variation — the standard run used a different border." "The factory made fewer than 400 of these in the cobalt colorway before switching to green." "This is the first year of issue — the design changed the following year and has never been this clean again."

What does not work: "These are really rare." "You don't see this very often." "This is one of the hard-to-find ones." "Collectors love this."

If CollectiblesBot or AntiqueBot returns a population report number, census count, or production run figure, that number belongs in the script. Numbers are credible. Adjectives are not.

When rarity is unknown: "I have not been able to find another one in this condition at current auction search. That is not a claim — it is where my research sits right now. If you know of comparable examples, I want to hear about them."

This kind of intellectual honesty builds credibility with serious collectors faster than overstatement.

## Historical Context as Viral Hook

The most powerful hook in antique and collectible video is connecting the item to a moment in history that the viewer already cares about. The item becomes a time machine.

Identify the historical moment first. Then build the hook around the viewer's existing emotional investment in that moment.

Examples by category:

Civil War-era items: "This was made the same year the Battle of Gettysburg ended the Confederate advance into the North. Whoever owned this originally was alive for that."

World War II items: "This was manufactured in 1943 — the same year production of civilian goods was halted across the country. Most factories were making something for the war effort. This one kept going."

Space Age items: "This television was manufactured in 1969. The Apollo 11 moon landing was broadcast on sets exactly like this one. Someone watched Neil Armstrong step onto the moon on a screen like this."

Early American items: "This was made before the United States was 20 years old. The country had just had its first contested presidential election. Jefferson had just taken office. And someone was making this in their shop."

The historical hook works because the viewer already has emotional investment in the moment. The item becomes a physical connection to that investment. That emotional connection drives shares, saves, and comments — the algorithm signals that matter.

## How Collector Communities Respond to Video

Understanding the sharing behavior of each collector community shapes the script structure.

Card collectors share PSA grade reveals. The reveal moment — when the grade number appears — is the climax of the video. Build to it. Do not open with it. Show the raw card, discuss the condition, explain what grade you are hoping for, and then reveal the submission result or the estimated grade. The suspense before the reveal is what gets shared.

Antique collectors share maker mark discoveries. The close-up shot of the mark — the Stickley decal, the Rookwood flame, the Tiffany Studios stamp, the silver hallmark — is the climax. Build to it. Describe the piece, discuss what you suspected, and then show the mark discovery moment. Reactions in the comment section generate algorithm engagement.

Coin collectors share die variety finds. The detail shot through a loupe or under magnification, revealing a doubled die or a repunched mintmark, is the climax. Build anticipation by explaining what you are looking for and why it matters.

Pottery and ceramic collectors share glaze and form appreciation. The script for this category should describe the glaze technically — flambe, crystalline, standard, iris, mahogany — and show the piece in raking light that reveals the depth of the finish.

Paper ephemera and photograph collectors share context. A real photo postcard from 1910 becomes viral when the script identifies who might be in it, where it might have been taken, and what was happening in that town in that year.

## The Reveal Format

The reveal format is the TikTok structure that collector content thrives on. It is not a gimmick — it is the natural shape of the collector experience. Someone finds something, investigates it, and discovers what it is. The video recreates that experience.

Structure:

1. The find (5-10 seconds): Show the item as found or as purchased. Keep the camera back. Do not reveal details yet. "I want to show you what I picked up this week."

2. The investigation (20-40 seconds): Walk through the examination. Show the construction, the markings, the condition. Narrate what you are looking for and why. "The dovetailing is hand-cut. That tells me pre-1880 or a very high-end maker after that. The hardware is original — you can see where the screws have been tightened but never replaced. Now look at the bottom."

3. The reveal (5-10 seconds): The mark. The grade. The identification. The price comparable. Whatever the climax of the discovery is, deliver it with quiet confidence. Let the camera hold on it for a beat.

4. The CTA (3-5 seconds): Direct and specific. "DM me CHEST for price and dimensions."

This format is universally applicable across antiques, coins, cards, ceramics, paper, and most collectible categories. The investigation section length varies by category complexity. The reveal moment is always the climax.

## Category-Specific Script Notes

Furniture: Lead with construction technique (joinery type, wood species, finish). Reveal maker attribution last.

Ceramics and pottery: Lead with form and glaze. Reveal factory and artist last.

Silver and metalwork: Lead with weight and hallmarks. Reveal maker and date last.

Coins: Lead with grade and eye appeal. Reveal variety (if any) as the climax.

Sports and trading cards: Lead with the player, the year, the set. Reveal the grade as the climax.

Comics: Lead with the issue significance (first appearance, key cover). Reveal the grade as the climax.

Toys and cast iron: Lead with the rarity of the specific example. Reveal the condition and completeness assessment as the climax.

Photography and paper: Lead with the historical context. Reveal the identification of the subject or location as the climax.
