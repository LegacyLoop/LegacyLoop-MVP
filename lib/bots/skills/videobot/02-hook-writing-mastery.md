# Hook Writing Mastery

## The 3-Second Rule

Short-form video lives and dies in the first three seconds. TikTok's internal data consistently shows that videos losing more than 40 percent of viewers in the first three seconds almost never recover algorithmically. The platform penalizes poor retention by reducing distribution. This is not a stylistic preference — it is the fundamental law of short-form video economics.

Every script VideoBot generates must lead with a hook that gives a viewer a specific, compelling reason to stop scrolling. Not a vague reason. Not a generally interesting reason. A precise, emotionally activating reason that is tailored to the item, the category, and the platform.

## Why Hooks Are Written First

The single most common script-writing mistake is writing the body of a script and then adding a hook at the beginning. This produces hooks that feel bolted on, disconnected, and weak.

The correct discipline: identify the single most compelling fact about this item, write the hook around that fact, and then build the body of the script to support what the hook promises. The hook is the contract with the viewer. The body and reveal are the delivery on that contract.

If the hook promises mystery, the body must deliver a satisfying resolution. If the hook promises surprising value, the body must reveal and justify that value. If the hook promises a great story, the body must tell it.

Never bury the hook. Never open with "So today I wanted to share..." Never open with a greeting. Never open with context-setting that could come later. The viewer's attention is the most expensive resource in the transaction — spend it on the hook.

## Hook Pattern One: The Curiosity Gap

The curiosity gap is the space between what the viewer knows and what they want to know. Effective curiosity gap hooks create an information asymmetry that the viewer must resolve by watching the video.

Examples for estate and antique items:

- "Wait — is this actually worth $10,000?"
- "I had no idea what this was until I looked it up."
- "The appraiser went quiet when she saw this."
- "There is something written on the back of this that changes everything."
- "My antique dealer friend told me never to sell this. Here is why."

The curiosity gap works because the human brain has a documented compulsion to close open loops. A question asked directly at a viewer creates cognitive dissonance that the viewer resolves by continuing to watch.

Strength calibration: the curiosity gap hook is strongest for items with surprising value, hidden provenance, or unusual markings. It is less effective for common items with straightforward value propositions.

## Hook Pattern Two: The Pattern Interrupt

A pattern interrupt is any hook — visual, verbal, or both — that breaks the viewer's passive scrolling mental state and forces active attention. The viewer's thumb was about to move; the pattern interrupt stops it.

Verbal pattern interrupt examples:

- "Everyone told me to throw this away."
- "I almost donated this for $5."
- "This looked like trash. It was not trash."
- "Nobody at the estate sale touched this. That was their mistake."
- "I paid $12 for this at a flea market. Collector price: $1,800."

The pattern interrupt works because it creates a narrative injustice — a mismatch between how the item was perceived and how it actually should have been perceived. Viewers want to see that injustice resolved. They want the item to be vindicated. They want the person who dismissed it to be proven wrong.

This hook pattern has extremely high completion rates because the emotional arc (underdog item proves its worth) is deeply satisfying.

## Hook Pattern Three: The Reveal

The reveal hook begins in medias res — the viewer is dropped directly into the moment of discovery or the moment of reveal, with no preamble.

Examples:

- "I found this in my grandmother's attic."
- "It was in a box labeled MISC at an estate sale."
- "This came out of a house built in 1887."
- "The woman selling it had no idea what she had."
- "This has been in the same family since 1923. They are selling it now."

The reveal hook works for estate and antique content because the origin story of an object is inherently interesting to collectors. Provenance is value. The discovery narrative replicates the thrill of the find that drives the entire antique and collectible market.

Strength calibration: most effective for items with genuine provenance or a compelling discovery context. Can be used even for items found at yard sales — the yard sale discovery narrative is its own genre with an established audience.

## Hook Pattern Four: The Challenge

The challenge hook invites viewer participation by posing a direct question that the viewer wants to answer before the video answers it for them.

Examples:

- "Can you guess what this is worth?"
- "What year do you think this was made?"
- "Would you know what this is if you saw it at a yard sale?"
- "Comment your guess before the reveal."
- "Most people have no idea what this is. Do you?"

The challenge hook drives comments because viewers answer the question in the comments section before the video reveals the answer. Comments are one of the strongest algorithmic signals on every major platform. A challenge hook that generates 40 comments from people guessing the value dramatically outperforms a polished hook that generates zero comments.

This hook is particularly effective for unusual antiques, obscure collectibles, and any item whose category is not immediately obvious from appearance.

## Hook Pattern Five: The Authority Hook

The authority hook establishes credibility in the first sentence and uses that credibility as the hook itself.

Examples:

- "As a 30-year antique dealer, I have rarely seen one of these in this condition."
- "This is one of only three items I have ever refused to put a price on."
- "I have been appraising furniture since 1994. This stopped me."
- "In 20 years of estate sales, this is the most undervalued thing I have ever bought."

The authority hook works because it immediately answers the viewer's implicit question: "Why should I listen to this person about this item?" Establishing domain expertise in the first sentence converts casual viewers into attentive learners.

Strength calibration: highly effective for high-value items, rare pieces, and auction-quality collectibles. Less effective for common household items where authority framing feels disproportionate.

## Category-Specific Hook Formulas

Antiques (pre-1900 objects):
- Lead with age: "This was made before the Civil War."
- Lead with rarity: "Only a handful of these survive in this condition."
- Lead with era connection: "This belonged to the generation that built this country."

Collectibles (graded cards, coins, stamps, comics):
- Lead with grade: "PSA 9. That number changes everything."
- Lead with scarcity: "Population report: 12 exist at this grade worldwide."
- Lead with market: "This card sold for $4,200 at auction last month."

Vehicles:
- Lead with numbers: "Numbers-matching. That is the only two words that matter here."
- Lead with barn find status: "It has been sitting in a barn since 1971."
- Lead with documentation: "It has the original window sticker. Still in the glove box."

General estate items:
- Lead with neighbor narrative: "My neighbor was throwing this away."
- Lead with price mismatch: "Goodwill price: $8. Actual value: $340."
- Lead with recognition trigger: "Your grandmother had one of these."

## The Hook-First Writing Discipline in Practice

When VideoBot receives item data — category, description, AI analysis results, pricing estimate, photos — the first output is always the hook. Not the full script. The hook.

Evaluate three candidate hooks. Score each on: specificity (generic hooks underperform specific ones), emotional activation (curiosity, surprise, nostalgia, or injustice), and platform fit (TikTok rewards faster/more unhinged hooks; Facebook rewards warmer/more trust-based hooks).

Select the highest-scoring hook. Build the script body to deliver on the hook's promise. Close with a CTA that is consistent with the emotional tone the hook established.

This is the only correct order of operations for short-form video script writing.
