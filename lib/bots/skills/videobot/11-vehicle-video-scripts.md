# Vehicle Video Scripts

## CarBot Integration: What the Data Means on Camera

CarBot returns structured vehicle data including matching-numbers status, Hagerty valuation tier, NHTSA recall count and resolution status, mileage analysis versus production-year average, regional market data, and title history. Each of these data points translates into specific video content.

Matching-numbers status: If CarBot confirms matching numbers, this is the lead. "Matching numbers" means the engine, transmission, and body all retain their original, factory-paired components. A numbers-matching classic is worth substantially more than an equivalent example with a replaced drivetrain, and buyers in this category know the difference. The script says it plainly: "CarBot confirmed matching numbers on the drivetrain. This car has never been apart."

Hagerty valuation tier: Hagerty classifies collector vehicles on a scale of #1 Concours through #6 Parts Car. If CarBot returns a Hagerty tier, name it. "Hagerty would rate this example at a strong #3 — it is a driver-quality car that presents well and is ready to use." Do not paraphrase Hagerty tiers in ways that inflate them. Say what the tier is and what it means.

NHTSA recall count and resolution: If CarBot returns open recalls, the script must acknowledge them. Buyers who discover undisclosed recalls after purchase lose trust in the seller and the platform. The script handles this directly: "NHTSA shows two recalls on this model year. Both have been addressed — I have the documentation." Or, when recalls are unresolved: "NHTSA shows one open recall. I want to be upfront about that. It is a safety item that should be addressed before regular use, and I have factored that into the price."

Mileage analysis: CarBot compares the vehicle's odometer reading against the production-year average mileage for that make and model. Below-average mileage is a selling point that deserves specific language: "For a 1987 truck, CarBot puts the average mileage at around 180,000 by now. This one shows 67,000. That is either genuine low mileage or a replaced cluster — and the service records support the odometer."

Regional market data: CarBot returns regional price variance when available. A vehicle that commands a premium in the sunbelt but less in the northeast, or vice versa, generates shipping inquiry. The script notes this when relevant: "These trucks bring more in the southwest where the rust-free examples are concentrated. This one is rust-free Maine, which is unusual."

## The Vehicle Video Hook Hierarchy

Not all vehicle selling points generate equal engagement. The hook hierarchy, ranked by viewer response and algorithm performance:

1. Barn find: A vehicle discovered in storage, barn, garage, or warehouse generates the highest organic engagement of any vehicle content category. The reveal moment — opening the doors, turning on the lights, showing the storage environment — is the hook. Lead with the discovery story.

2. Matching numbers: For collector and classic vehicle audiences, matching numbers is the second most powerful hook. The buyer who is searching for a numbers-matching example will stop scrolling.

3. Low mileage: Verified low mileage creates urgency. Time is working against low-mileage vehicles that sit unused. The buyer who wants one knows they need to act.

4. One owner: Single-ownership history implies documented care and known history. For the buyer who dreads hidden damage and deferred maintenance, one owner is a trust signal.

5. Rare color or option: Factory rare colors and option combinations — a four-speed on a model that rarely received one, a color that was discontinued mid-year, a regional dealer option — appeal to completists and concours competitors.

6. Clean title: For vehicles in the daily driver and late-model used categories, clean title is a primary selling point. The audience is larger and less specialized than the collector audience.

7. Recent service: Fresh timing belt, recent major service, new tires — these are buying triggers for practical buyers who do not want to inherit deferred maintenance.

## Walk-Around Format

The vehicle walk-around is the standard format for vehicle video content. It mirrors how a buyer inspects a vehicle in person, which is why it works. The buyer watching already has a mental script for what they want to see. The video fulfills that script.

Walk-around sequence:

Driver-side front quarter: Show the nose and the front left corner. State the paint code if known. Note any imperfections at this angle.

Front: Grille, badging, headlights. For classic vehicles, show the chrome condition.

Passenger-side front quarter: Show the passenger corner. Compare any paint variation to the driver side.

Passenger-side full length: Pan slowly from front to rear. Let the line of the car read.

Passenger-side rear quarter: Trunk lid, tail panel, rear glass.

Rear: Tailights, bumper, exhaust tips. If the vehicle has a tow hitch, show it.

Driver-side full length: Close the loop.

Engine bay: Open the hood with the camera already running. State what you are looking at — engine code, date stamps, original hoses or replacements, cleanliness, any modifications.

Interior driver: Door open, seat condition, dash, headliner, door card. State original vs replaced components.

Odometer close-up: Hold the camera on the odometer reading for a full second. Do not cut away before the number is clearly visible.

Interior passenger: Any condition notes from this angle.

Trunk: Open, show floor, spare, jack.

Cold start: See the dedicated section below.

## Classic vs Daily Driver Framing

These are different audiences with different purchase motivations. The script frame must match the vehicle.

Classic and collector vehicles get the "look what I found" treatment. The voice is discovery, preservation, and appreciation. The buyer is looking for something specific, has been patient, and recognizes what they are seeing. The script assumes knowledge. "The date codes on the block match the build sheet. The carburetor is a correct replacement — not the original, but the correct replacement date range." This buyer wants to be respected as a peer, not sold to.

Daily driver and late-model used vehicles get the "here is why this is a smart buy" treatment. The voice is practical value, reliability, and transparency. The buyer is making a transportation decision with a real budget. The script assumes nothing about prior knowledge and leads with total cost of ownership, service history, and immediate usability. "New tires last spring, timing belt done at 85,000, clean Carfax, no accidents, ready to drive."

The mistake is running a daily driver script on a classic or a classic script on a daily driver. A 2018 Honda CR-V does not benefit from provenance storytelling. A 1967 Camaro does not benefit from "ready to drive today" framing — the buyer already assumes it will need work.

## Bring a Trailer and Auction House Language

For classic, collector, and enthusiast vehicles, citing recent auction results — particularly Bring a Trailer sold listings — is the most credible pricing reference available.

Bring a Trailer (BaT) language: "A comparable example — same year, similar color, automatic transmission — sold on Bring a Trailer last month for $34,500. This one has the four-speed, which typically brings more. I have it priced at $38,000."

Mecum and Barrett-Jackson language: "The last Mecum sale on a matching-numbers example of this model brought $62,000. This car is not at that level — it is a driver, not a concours car — but it is priced accordingly."

When no recent auction comparable exists: "I have not found a recent BaT sale on an automatic in this color. The closest is a four-speed silver that brought $28,000 eighteen months ago. I am basing the ask on that result and the current market trend in the Mustang forum community."

Do not cite auction results without verifying them. Collectors in the comment section will check. An inaccurate BaT citation destroys credibility faster than no citation at all.

## The Cold Start Hook

Starting the engine on camera is the single most engaging moment in any vehicle video. It is more compelling than any walk-around angle, any interior shot, any odometer reading. The cold start is the proof of life.

The cold start works because it answers the question every buyer has but rarely asks directly: does it actually run? All the photos and descriptions in the world are secondary to the moment the engine catches and settles.

Script structure for the cold start hook:

Option A (open with the start): Begin the video with the camera already on the starter turn. "Cold, sitting for two weeks. No choke, no warm-up. Here it is." Catch the startup and let it idle for five to ten seconds. Then cut to the walk-around.

Option B (close with the start): Walk around first, build the case for the vehicle, and end with the cold start as the payoff. "You have seen it. Here is the last thing I want to show you." The cold start becomes the emotional close before the CTA.

For vehicles that require starting ritual — choke procedure, priming, warm-up before running smoothly — narrate the procedure and explain why. Buyers who know the vehicle recognize the correct procedure as a sign of knowledge. Buyers who do not know the vehicle learn something. Both outcomes build trust.

## What Car Buyers Want to See

The practical checklist for vehicle video content:

Exterior walk-around with all four corners visible. Buyers who cannot see the rear quarter panels assume the worst.

Engine start and idle. See above.

Interior condition including seats, dash, headliner, and carpet. A strong exterior with a destroyed interior is a common disappointment. Show the interior honestly.

Tire condition and tread depth. Tire replacement cost affects the offer. Show the tires.

Odometer reading in a close, steady shot. Blurry or cut-away odometer shots generate suspicion.

VIN plate visible and readable. Dashboard VIN plate on a clear shot. This is a trust signal for buyers doing their due diligence.

Any known imperfections shown directly. A rust spot acknowledged on camera is less damaging than a rust spot discovered at pickup. Honesty about condition defects increases purchase confidence because it signals that the seller is not hiding anything.

## LOCAL PICKUP ONLY CTA Requirement

Every vehicle video script must include "LOCAL PICKUP ONLY" in the call to action. This is a platform-level requirement for vehicle sales content across all LegacyLoop video outputs.

Vehicle sales involve physical inspection, test drives, title transfer, and transaction logistics that are not compatible with remote purchase and shipping in most cases. Setting this expectation in the video prevents wasted inquiry from buyers who are not in the region.

CTA format: "Local pickup only — serious inquiries, DM me VIN for the Carfax and current location."

For vehicles that can be transported by enclosed or open carrier: "Buyer is welcome to arrange transport at their expense. I can provide access for a transporter inspection. DM me TRUCK for details and current location."

Vehicles must not be presented as available for standard shipping under any circumstances without explicit confirmation of a compliant shipping arrangement.
