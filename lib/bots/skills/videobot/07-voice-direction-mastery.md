# Voice Direction Mastery

## Why Voice Profile Selection Is Not Optional

The narration voice is not decoration. It is the primary trust signal in a video about a physical item the buyer cannot inspect in person. A voice that is too casual for a $3,000 item signals that the seller does not understand the item's value. A voice that is too formal for a $40 yard sale find signals inauthenticity and creates cognitive dissonance.

The correct voice match — calibrated to item value, item category, platform, and emotional context — is one of the highest-leverage decisions VideoBot makes in the script generation process. Two scripts with identical words but different voice profiles will produce meaningfully different conversion outcomes.

VideoBot selects from five ElevenLabs voice profiles. Each profile has a defined character, a primary use case, a price-point sweet spot, and platform affinities. Understanding these profiles at the level of craft — not just as labels — is essential for generating video scripts that convert.

## Voice Profile One: WARM (Sarah)

Character: Sarah sounds like a museum audio guide narrator — smooth, trustworthy, and knowledgeable without being cold or clinical. She communicates care for the subject matter. The listener feels that she genuinely values and respects what she is describing.

Primary use cases: Antique furniture, silver, porcelain, jewelry, estate items with personal or family history, items where the emotional context of the object matters as much as the financial value. Items where the buyer needs to feel trust before they can feel excitement.

Price-point sweet spot: $200 to $2,000. Below $200, the warmth and care feel disproportionate to the item's significance. Above $2,000, the collector or high-value buyer typically expects more authority and expertise signaling than warmth alone provides.

Platform affinities: Facebook (warmth aligns with the trust-first Facebook conversion model), Instagram Reels (warmth matches the aspirational but accessible Reels tone).

Pacing: 110-120 words per minute. Measured and deliberate — room for the words to land.

Sample direction note: "Deliver the opening hook with genuine warmth — this is someone sharing something they find beautiful, not someone pitching a product. The condition description should feel like care, not a checklist."

## Voice Profile Two: ENERGETIC (Josh)

Character: Josh sounds like a knowledgeable friend who just found something incredible and cannot wait to tell you about it. The energy is genuine, not performative. He is enthusiastic because the item deserves enthusiasm, not because he is following a script.

Primary use cases: Trending collectibles (sneakers, trading cards, vinyl records, vintage gaming), items with strong youth market appeal, finds where the discovery story is the primary hook, any item where the viewer's first emotional response should be excitement rather than trust.

Price-point sweet spot: Any price. Josh works from $15 thrift finds to $1,500 sneakers because the energy is about the find, not the price. The word "incredible" landing at the right moment matters more than the price tag.

Platform affinities: TikTok (energetic matches Gen Z's "no way" energy expectations), Instagram Reels (energetic works in the 25-35 demographic for high-dopamine content categories).

Pacing: 130-145 words per minute. Fast enough to feel urgent and alive, but not so fast that detail is lost.

Sample direction note: "The value reveal at 0:18 should land with genuine surprise in the delivery — a slight pause before the number, then the number itself with forward energy. This is the emotional peak of the video. Let it be the peak."

## Voice Profile Three: PROFESSIONAL (Daniel)

Character: Daniel sounds like a Sotheby's narration — measured, authoritative, and precise. He communicates domain expertise through tone as much as through content. The listener immediately understands they are hearing from someone with deep knowledge of the category.

Primary use cases: High-value collectibles ($1,000+), auction-quality antiques, rare pieces where the collector buyer needs authority signals before they engage seriously, items where the primary buyer is an experienced collector rather than a casual buyer.

Price-point sweet spot: $1,000 and above. Below $1,000, the auction-house authority can feel excessive and may actually signal that the seller is overselling a modest item. Above $5,000, Daniel is the only appropriate voice profile for VideoBot scripts.

Platform affinities: YouTube Shorts (authoritative tone matches the expert-sharing-knowledge Shorts format), Facebook (builds trust with experienced collectors in the 45-65 demographic).

Pacing: 100-115 words per minute. Measured and precise — each sentence feels considered.

Sample direction note: "The provenance section (0:22-0:38) should be delivered with the quiet confidence of someone who has verified this information personally. Not slow — just deliberate. This is the part of the script that separates serious buyers from casual browsers."

## Voice Profile Four: CASUAL (Bill)

Character: Bill sounds like a knowledgeable neighbor who happened to find something interesting and is telling you about it over the fence. The tone is relaxed and unpretentious. There is warmth but not sentimentality, knowledge but not expertise-signaling.

Primary use cases: Everyday household items priced under $100, garage sale and flea market finds, items where the primary selling point is practical utility combined with good price, content aimed at budget-conscious buyers or casual browsers rather than collectors.

Price-point sweet spot: Under $100. Bill's casual tone signals appropriate value expectations. Using Bill for a $500 item signals that the seller may be undervaluing it.

Platform affinities: Facebook (casual matches the conversational Facebook tone for lower-value items), TikTok (casual works for the "my neighbor was throwing this away" discovery narrative).

Pacing: 100-110 words per minute. Conversational — as if speaking to one person rather than to an audience.

Sample direction note: "This script should feel like a conversation, not a presentation. Bill is telling a friend about a find, not announcing a sale. Keep the energy level flat but engaged throughout — this is not an excited voice, it is a pleased one."

## Voice Profile Five: STORYTELLER (Bella)

Character: Bella sounds like a documentary narrator — evocative, precise, and fully committed to the emotional truth of the story she is telling. She draws the listener into a world and makes them feel the weight of what the item represents.

Primary use cases: Items with powerful provenance or personal history, estate items where the seller wants to honor the original owner's story, heirlooms, items from specific historical periods where the cultural context adds significant value, and any content where the emotional resonance of the object's story is the primary selling mechanism.

Price-point sweet spot: Any price. Bella is effective for a $30 World War II-era letter as much as for a $5,000 period piece. The criterion for Bella is not price but story depth — does this item have a story worth telling with full narrative commitment?

Platform affinities: Instagram Reels (storytelling aligns with Reels' SETUP-TENSION-RESOLUTION arc), Facebook (emotional storytelling works well with Facebook's 35-65 audience, especially for estate items with personal history).

Pacing: 105-125 words per minute. Variable — slower on emotional beats, slightly faster through factual exposition.

Sample direction note: "The opening line should be delivered as if Bella is beginning a story she has been wanting to tell for a long time. This is not a product announcement. This is a story with a beginning, a middle, and an end. Honor that structure in the delivery."

## Category-to-Voice Matching Rules

Antique furniture and decorative arts: Sarah (Warm) for under $2,000; Daniel (Professional) for over $2,000.
Sterling silver and fine jewelry: Sarah (Warm) for personal jewelry; Daniel (Professional) for significant pieces.
Trading cards, sneakers, and sports collectibles: Josh (Energetic) always.
Vinyl records and vintage audio: Josh (Energetic) for popular artists; Daniel (Professional) for rare pressings.
Estate jewelry with personal history: Bella (Storyteller) when provenance is the primary value driver.
Vintage tools and industrial items: Bill (Casual) for under $100; Daniel (Professional) for rare examples.
Military and historical items: Bella (Storyteller) always.
Vehicles and automotive items: Daniel (Professional) for high-value; Josh (Energetic) for discovery content.
General household items: Bill (Casual) for under $50; Sarah (Warm) for $50-$200.

## How Narration Tone Affects Trust and Conversion Rate

The mechanism by which narration tone affects conversion is not mysterious — it is the same social trust calibration that operates in face-to-face commerce. A seller's tone signals whether they understand what they are selling, whether they are honest about its value, and whether they are speaking to the buyer as an equal.

A voice that is too casual for a high-value item signals either that the seller does not know the item's true value (making the buyer feel they may be missing important information) or that the seller is misrepresenting the value (making the buyer feel they may be being played). Either perception kills conversion.

A voice that is too formal for a low-value item signals that the seller is overselling — creating inflated expectations that the item cannot meet. This generates returns, disputes, and negative reviews.

The correct voice match creates a moment of recognition in the viewer: "Yes, this person understands exactly what this is." That recognition is the foundation of conversion. Everything else in the script builds on it.
