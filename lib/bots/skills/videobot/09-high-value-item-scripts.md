# High-Value Item Video Scripts

## Trigger Threshold

This pack activates when an item's estimated value exceeds $500 as reported by PriceBot or AntiqueBot. When the OpenAI secondary fires on the high_value trigger, the final script output becomes a dual-opinion document: one voice from the collector-audience frame, one voice from the investor-audience frame. The seller chooses which voice to publish, or publishes both versions across different platforms.

## Audience Framing: Collector vs Investor

These are two distinct buyer psychologies and they require two distinct scripts.

The collector wants to know: Is this real? Where has it been? Why is this piece significant? What will owning it mean to their collection? Collectors buy with their heart and justify with their head. Script language for collectors leads with story, authentication, and rarity. The word "rare" appears only when it is verifiably true. The word "museum-quality" appears only when it can be defended.

The investor wants to know: What did comparable pieces sell for at auction? What is the trajectory of this category? What is the liquidity risk? What is the replacement value for insurance purposes? Investors buy with their head and feel the acquisition pride later. Script language for investors leads with data, comparables, and market positioning.

High-value video scripts must serve one audience per script. Trying to serve both simultaneously produces a script that converts neither.

## Quiet Confidence Voice

This is the governing principle for all $500+ scripts. The voice is:

- Authoritative without being arrogant
- Specific without being clinical
- Measured without being slow
- Confident without being breathless

What quiet confidence sounds like: "This is a signed Stickley side table, original finish, hardware intact. Period Arts and Crafts, circa 1905. Comparable pieces at Rago have brought between four thousand and seven thousand dollars at auction. This one is priced at forty-five hundred."

What quiet confidence does not sound like: "Oh my gosh you guys, I found the most AMAZING piece, this is going to blow your mind, you will not BELIEVE what this is worth."

Breathless excitement signals to the serious buyer that the seller does not understand what they have. Quiet confidence signals that the seller knows exactly what they have and is not desperate.

## Authenticity and Provenance Emphasis

High-value items sell on documentation, history, and verifiable claims. Never hype.

Authenticity signals to integrate into scripts when available:
- Maker marks, signatures, labels, stamps, hallmarks — name them specifically
- Provenance chain: estate of origin, family lineage, prior collection ownership
- Prior exhibition or publication history
- Prior appraisal documentation (name the appraiser and approximate date if available)
- Original receipts, certificates of authenticity, correspondence related to the item

Script language for provenance: "This piece comes from the estate of a prominent Portland physician who acquired it in the 1960s. The original purchase receipt accompanies the lot."

Script language for authenticity markers: "The maker's stamp on the underside reads Tiffany Studios New York with the pattern number 1463. This is consistent with the Peony lamp shade produced between 1906 and 1920."

When provenance is unknown: "The piece has no documented provenance, but the construction and patina are consistent with the period. A collector or dealer would want an independent assessment before attributing."

Never fabricate provenance. Never imply documentation that does not exist. Serious buyers verify.

## Auction House Reference Integration

When AntiqueBot or PriceBot returns comparable auction results, integrate them with specificity. Vague comparables undermine credibility. Specific comparables build it.

Correct: "A similar piece, also in original finish, sold at Skinner in January for twelve thousand dollars. This one is priced below that result."

Incorrect: "These kinds of things sell for a lot at auction."

Auction house references that carry weight with collector and investor audiences:
- Sotheby's, Christie's, Bonhams (international fine art and antiques)
- Rago/Wright (design, decorative arts, modern)
- Skinner (American antiques, Asian art, jewelry)
- Heritage Auctions (coins, comics, cards, memorabilia, wine)
- Mecum, Barrett-Jackson (vehicles)
- Hakes (pop culture, political memorabilia)
- Stack's Bowers (coins, currency)

If PriceBot data includes a sold result from any of these houses, cite it in the script by name, result, and approximate date.

## Insurance and Replacement Value Framing

For items estimated above $2,000, the script should include a recommendation that the buyer obtain a professional appraisal for insurance purposes. This is not a disclaimer — it is a signal of legitimacy that serious buyers appreciate.

Script language: "At this price point, most buyers will want an independent appraisal for insurance coverage. We recommend finding a certified appraiser through the American Society of Appraisers or the Appraisers Association of America."

This framing accomplishes two things: it signals that the seller is serious about the item's value, and it pre-qualifies buyers who are ready to make a real investment rather than a casual purchase.

## How Video Framing Affects Final Sale Price

A well-crafted video for a $5,000 item can shift the perceived value and final sale price by 15-25%. This is not conjecture. It reflects buyer psychology around trust signals.

A video that looks professional, sounds authoritative, and demonstrates genuine knowledge of the item creates a trust premium. The buyer who watches a quiet, confident 90-second script on a signed Stickley table pays closer to ask than the buyer who sees the same table in a blurry photo with a generic description.

The specific elements that create trust premium in high-value video:
- Steady camera, good lighting, close-up detail shots
- Voice that does not waver or hedge when discussing price
- Specific auction comparables named aloud
- Physical handling of the piece that demonstrates familiarity
- Visible maker marks, signatures, or labels shown on camera

## Dual-Opinion Output Format

When the OpenAI secondary triggers on high_value, VideoBot returns two complete scripts:

Script A (Collector Voice): Story-first. Opens with the historical or artistic significance of the piece. Provenance and maker identity are the hook. Price is revealed at the end as a secondary consideration. CTA invites serious inquiry, not impulse buys.

Script B (Investor Voice): Data-first. Opens with the auction comparable or market trajectory. Price is positioned relative to replacement value and recent sold results. CTA frames the purchase as a time-sensitive market opportunity.

Both scripts use the quiet confidence voice. Neither uses breathless excitement. The platform determines which script to lead with: TikTok and Instagram favor the collector voice, LinkedIn and email favor the investor voice.

## Example: Script A (Collector Voice)

"Gustav Stickley designed this side table in 1905. He believed furniture should be honest — no veneers, no ornamentation that served no function. This piece survived 120 years with its original finish intact. The red decal under the lower shelf confirms the Eastwood, New York factory. Comparable examples have sold at Rago for five to eight thousand dollars. This one is priced at forty-five hundred. DM me TABLE for details."

## Example: Script B (Investor Voice)

"Arts and Crafts furniture has held its auction value through three recessions. A signed Stickley side table in original finish sold at Rago last fall for sixty-two hundred. This example is in comparable condition, also in original finish, with the factory decal present. Asking forty-five hundred — below the most recent comparable result. First serious inquiry gets first consideration. DM me TABLE."
