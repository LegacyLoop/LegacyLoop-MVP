# Facebook Video Standards

## Facebook's Unique Position in the Estate Sale Ecosystem

Facebook is not the platform where most short-form video trends originate, but it is the platform where estate sale transactions actually happen. The Facebook Marketplace and estate sale community groups are where buyers and sellers in the 35-65 demographic conduct real commerce. A video on TikTok might reach 10,000 people; a video shared into the right Facebook group might reach 500 people who are actively looking to buy this specific category of item today.

VideoBot treats Facebook video as a conversion tool more than a discovery tool. The audience is smaller but the purchase intent is higher. A collector in a "Victorian Antiques for Sale" Facebook group who sees a compelling video is at a different point in the buying funnel than a TikTok user who stumbled onto the same content.

This distinction shapes every element of the Facebook script: the tone is warmer, the pacing is slower, the trust signals are more explicit, and the CTAs are more transactional.

## The 45-Second Optimal Length

Facebook's internal data on native video engagement shows consistent drop-off after 45 seconds for organic (non-boosted) content in most categories. VideoBot targets 45 seconds for Facebook video scripts. At a conversational narration pace of approximately 100-110 words per minute, this translates to a word budget of 75-82 words.

Unlike TikTok's rigid 30-second constraint, Facebook allows more flexibility. A compelling estate story with strong emotional resonance may justify 60-75 seconds if the narrative sustains viewer interest. The test: would cutting to 45 seconds require removing something that materially affects the viewer's decision to engage or purchase? If yes, keep the length. If no, cut.

## The Facebook Demographic: 35-65

Facebook's core active demographic is 35-65, with the highest engagement in the 45-60 range. This audience was not raised on internet culture. They are not fluent in meme language. They are not moved by "unhinged" enthusiasm. They are moved by trust, authenticity, and clear communication.

This audience is also the primary estate sale demographic — both sellers and buyers. Many are liquidating deceased relatives' possessions. Many are collectors who have been accumulating knowledge and items for decades. Many have deep expertise in specific categories and can identify inauthenticity immediately.

Language calibration for Facebook's 35-65 demographic:

Preferred: Clear sentences. No jargon without explanation. Warm and conversational. Story-forward over data-forward (though data is welcome when grounded in a relatable context). "This belonged to my grandmother" over "Provenance: family estate."

Avoid: Internet slang and neologisms that feel out of place ("bussin," "no cap," "hits different"). Excessive exclamation points and caps-lock energy. Condescension toward older buyers. Assumptions that the viewer knows what an item's category-specific terminology means.

The Facebook viewer has more patience than the TikTok viewer — but this patience is conditional. They will watch longer if they are engaged and feel respected. They will stop watching immediately if they feel the video is not made for them or is talking down to them.

## Silent Autoplay: The First 3 Seconds Without Sound

Facebook videos autoplay silently in the feed. A viewer scrolling through their Facebook feed sees the first 3 seconds of the video before they decide to tap for audio. This creates a design constraint that is more demanding than TikTok or Reels: the first 3 seconds of the video must tell a compelling story with zero audio.

This means: the item must be clearly visible and visually interesting in the opening frame. Any critical hook information must appear as on-screen text overlay within the first 3 seconds. The visual composition must be compelling enough that the viewer taps for audio.

VideoBot Facebook scripts include mandatory visual direction notes:

- Opening frame: item prominently displayed, clearly lit, with visual context that establishes its character
- Text overlay within 0-3 seconds: the most compelling single fact about the item in 6 words or fewer
- Example: "Found in a 1924 farmhouse. Value: $1,800."
- Example: "One of only 200 made. Estate sale price: $45."
- Example: "My grandmother's ring. Appraiser's estimate: $4,000."

The text overlay functions as the hook for silent viewers. If it is compelling enough, they tap for audio. If they tap for audio, completion rates climb dramatically and Facebook's algorithm distributes the video more broadly.

## Facebook Groups as Distribution Infrastructure

Facebook Groups are the most powerful organic distribution mechanism on the platform for estate sale and antique content. Shares to relevant groups amplify reach without requiring ad spend.

The most valuable group categories for estate and antique content:

- "[City/State] Estate Sales" groups — direct buyer audience, high purchase intent
- "Antique Collectors" and category-specific collector groups — expert audience, high engagement
- "Facebook Marketplace" and buying/selling groups — transaction-ready audience
- "Vintage and Antique Lovers" communities — enthusiast audience, strong engagement and shares

VideoBot scripts include group-distribution optimized language — content that speaks directly to the collector community in a way that makes group members want to share it with other group members:

"For anyone in the antique collecting community — this is one of the clearest examples I have seen of how underprice these can be at estate sales."

"If you have friends who collect early American pottery, share this with them — this is exactly the category that gets overlooked."

## The Facebook Trust Framework

Trust is the foundation of Facebook commerce. The Facebook audience has been burned by fake listings, misrepresented conditions, and dishonest sellers. The video script must actively build trust, not assume it.

Trust signals VideoBot incorporates into Facebook scripts:

Physical evidence shown on camera: maker's marks, hallmarks, documentation, original packaging, original receipts. These are shown, not described.

Honest condition disclosure: if there is a crack, a chip, a repair, or wear, the script acknowledges it clearly and matter-of-factly. Hiding condition issues destroys trust when discovered; disclosing them builds trust when acknowledged.

Specific knowledge demonstrated: the narrator demonstrates genuine knowledge of the item's category, era, and market. This signals expertise and authenticity.

Location and context: "This came out of an estate in Kennebunkport, Maine, after a family of four generations" is more credible than "estate item in great condition." The specific, verifiable detail builds trust.

## The Facebook CTA Architecture

Facebook's algorithm rewards specific engagement actions, in this order: shares to groups (strongest), comments, reactions, and saves. VideoBot Facebook CTAs are engineered to trigger the highest-value engagement actions.

Tier 1 CTA (comment-trigger): "Comment INTERESTED below and I will send you full details and more photos." This generates comments (high algorithmic value) and creates a private message sales funnel.

Tier 2 CTA (share-trigger): "Share this with someone who loves antiques." This generates shares (highest algorithmic value) and distributes the video to new audiences organically.

Tier 3 CTA (reaction-trigger): "Click 'Wow' if you recognize what this is." This generates high-quality reactions (the Wow reaction is algorithmically more valuable than a standard Like).

Tier 4 CTA (direct-to-transaction): "Click the listing link in the comments to make an offer." This is the lowest algorithmic value CTA but has the highest purchase-intent conversion.

Every Facebook script uses exactly one primary CTA from this hierarchy, selected based on the item's price point and the seller's goal: algorithm reach (use Tier 1 or 2) vs. direct conversion (use Tier 4).

## What Makes Facebook Video Convert from Viewer to Buyer

The Facebook conversion chain differs from TikTok/Reels: discovery on Facebook is more social (shared by a trusted friend or community member) and less algorithmic (served by an impersonal platform). This social discovery context means the trust transfer from the person who shared the video to the viewer is already partially complete before the video begins.

The video's job on Facebook is to complete that trust transfer and make the path to purchase clear and easy. The viewer should finish the video knowing: what the item is, what its condition is, what it is worth, and exactly how to contact the seller or view the listing. Any ambiguity in the purchase path increases friction and reduces conversion.

A Facebook video that generates 50 comments asking "how much?" has failed as a conversion tool, because the price was not stated clearly in the video. State the price clearly. State the next step clearly. Remove friction.
