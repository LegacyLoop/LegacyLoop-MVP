# Estate Item Photography

## The Tone Principle: Help a Neighbor

Estate photography coaching starts with tone. The sellers PhotoBot serves in this context are often
older adults navigating technology they did not grow up with, in emotional circumstances — a parent's
home, a lifetime of accumulated belongings, a transition that carries real weight. The correct tone
is warm, patient, and specific. Never condescending. Never technical. Never in a hurry.

Think of it as helping a trusted neighbor. You know things they do not, but you explain them plainly,
you assume good faith, and you celebrate what they get right. Every instruction is given in plain
language. Every improvement suggestion is framed as help, not correction.

## Phone Photography for the First-Time Seller

Most estate sellers will use a smartphone camera. A modern smartphone camera is fully capable of
producing photos good enough to sell almost any estate item — the challenge is knowing how to use it.
These are the foundational techniques, stated in plain language without assumed technical knowledge.

### Tap to Focus

When you point your phone camera at an item, the phone may try to focus on the background or a wall
behind the item. Tap directly on the item on your screen before you take the photo. A small box or
circle will appear where you tapped. That tells the phone to make that spot sharp. Your item will
come out clear and the background can be slightly soft, which actually looks professional.

### Hold the Phone Steady

Blurry photos happen when the phone moves while the photo is being taken. Hold the phone with both
hands, elbows against your sides. Take a slow breath in, and press the shutter button gently as
you breathe out. If you have a helper, they can hold your arm steady. Some sellers find it easier
to rest the phone on a stack of books or a table while pointing it at the item.

### Turn Off the Flash

This is one of the most important phone photography tips. The built-in flash on most phones makes
photos look flat, creates harsh shadows, and often washes out the color and texture of the item.
Turn the flash off before you start. On most phones, there is a lightning bolt symbol at the top of
the camera screen. Tap it until it shows a line through it or the word "off."

Natural daylight gives you far better photos than the flash. Move the item near a window, or
photograph it outside in the shade (direct sunlight can create harsh shadows too).

### Clean the Camera Lens

This sounds simple but makes a meaningful difference. The camera lens on most phones collects
fingerprints and dust throughout the day. A smudged lens blurs every photo you take. Before you
start photographing items, wipe the camera lens gently with a soft cloth — a clean shirttail works
fine. Your photos will be noticeably sharper.

### Landscape for Furniture, Portrait for Tall Items

Turn the phone sideways (landscape mode) when photographing furniture, rugs, wide items, and
anything that is wider than it is tall. Keep the phone upright (portrait mode) for tall items like
lamps, floor clocks, standing mirrors, and vases. This simple adjustment means the item fills more
of the frame instead of appearing small with a lot of empty space around it.

## When Good Enough Is Good Enough

A clear phone photo taken in natural light near a window, with the flash turned off, will perform
better than a dark or blurry photo from a professional camera. Equipment does not determine photo
quality — light, steadiness, and composition do. A seller who follows the basics above and uses
their phone will produce photos that serve the listing well.

Do not let perfect be the enemy of good. An estate sale has time pressure, physical demands, and
emotional weight. Coaching that acknowledges this reality is more valuable than coaching that holds
sellers to a professional photography standard they cannot realistically meet.

## If the Seller Can Only Take 3 Photos: Prioritize These

When a seller can only photograph a limited number of photos, these three matter most:

One: A clear photo of the whole item from the front, in natural light, with nothing blocking the
view. The complete item, visible, well-lit. This is the hero photo.

Two: A close-up of any label, mark, signature, maker's stamp, or hallmark on the item. This single
photo can dramatically affect value, because it tells the AI and the buyer who made the item.

Three: A photo of the worst condition issue — a crack, a chip, a stain, a repair. Showing the
damage honestly protects the seller from disputes and shows buyers the item in full context.

With these three photos, a listing can be analyzed, priced, and published. More photos are always
better, but these three form a viable minimum.

## Coaching Children and Grandchildren Taking Photos on Behalf of the Seller

In many estate situations, a younger family member — a grandchild, a child, a family friend — takes
the photos on behalf of an older adult. This is common and practical. When coaching in this
scenario, acknowledge the dynamic positively. The younger person likely has more phone confidence,
and the goal is to direct that confidence toward the right shots.

Specific guidance for this scenario: show the younger photographer the item's maker mark or label
first, and make sure they photograph it. Walk them through the angle coverage — whole item, sides,
back, close-up marks, any damage. If they take a photo that is blurry or dark, ask them to retake
it immediately while they are still set up, before the light changes or the item is moved.

## The Practical Setup Checklist

Before the first photo is taken, run through this setup list:

Find a plain background. A white or light-colored wall, a clean bedsheet hung or draped flat, or
even the outside of a house on a cloudy day. The background does not need to be studio-quality —
it needs to be clear and not distracting.

Clear the area around the item. Remove other objects that are not part of the item being sold.
A lamp cord in the corner of the frame, a coffee cup on the table beside the item, a stack of
boxes in the background — all of these draw the buyer's eye away from the item and reduce the
perceived quality of the listing.

Choose daylight hours. Photograph near a window during the day. Morning and afternoon natural light
is ideal. Avoid photographing in the evening under artificial lights — the color temperature of
indoor lighting makes items look orange or yellow, which misrepresents colors and confuses buyers.

Two mandatory shots: At minimum, take one full-item photo (showing the entire item from the front)
and one close-up of any maker's mark, label, or identifying feature. These two photos give the
analysis system what it needs to do its job.

## Estate-Specific Photography Challenges and Solutions

### Cluttered Houses

Estate homes are often full. Items sit surrounded by other belongings. Moving an item to a clean
space for photography is always preferable, but not always possible.

When the item cannot be moved: use a white or neutral-colored sheet or towel placed behind the item
to create a clean backdrop. Even a folded sheet draped over nearby objects can separate the item
visually from the surrounding clutter. This simple technique transforms a cluttered-room photo into
a presentable listing photo.

### Large Furniture That Cannot Be Moved

For large pieces — armoires, dining tables, pianos, bookcases — photograph in place. The priority
is clear light and a clear view of the full item. Open curtains and blinds on nearby windows to
bring in natural light. Turn on room lights to supplement if needed. Photograph from enough
distance to show the entire piece in the frame. If the room is too cramped, photograph from the
doorway.

### Fragile Items That Should Not Be Handled

For fragile ceramics, glass, or items with unstable repairs, do not move the item more than
necessary. Set up the background and light around the item's location. Use natural window light
by repositioning the photographer's angle rather than the item's position. If the item must be
photographed on a surface that is not ideal, use a cloth backdrop placed on that surface under
the item. Photograph from multiple angles by moving around the item rather than moving the item.
