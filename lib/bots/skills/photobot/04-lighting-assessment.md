# Lighting Assessment

## Why Lighting Is the Foundation of Product Photography

Lighting is not decoration. It is the mechanism by which every other quality attribute of a product photograph is either revealed or concealed. A well-lit photo of a mediocre item will outperform a poorly lit photo of a premium item in nearly every measurable outcome — click rate, offer rate, and final sale price.

The reason is that human visual perception evolved to extract information about surface texture, depth, color, and material quality from reflected light. When lighting is poor, the brain cannot extract this information reliably, and the response is caution, not confidence. When lighting is excellent, the brain processes the item quickly and generates the specific quality assessments — "this looks well-made," "this looks clean," "this looks like what the seller says it is" — that precede purchase decisions.

## Natural vs Artificial Lighting

For resale photography, natural daylight is almost always superior to artificial lighting. The reasons are technical and consistent.

Natural daylight, particularly on an overcast day or in open shade, is an extremely large, diffused light source. Large, diffused light sources produce soft shadows with gradual transitions. Soft shadows reveal surface texture and three-dimensional form without creating harsh dark zones that obscure detail. Overcast daylight is also spectrally broad and roughly balanced, meaning colors in the photo will closely match colors in person.

Direct sunlight is the exception. Direct noon sunlight is a small, hard light source that produces sharp, deep shadows, specular highlights that blow out surface detail on reflective items, and high contrast that obscures both shadow and highlight detail simultaneously. Direct sunlight at any angle other than golden hour is one of the worst conditions for product photography.

Artificial lighting fails primarily because most available artificial light sources — incandescent bulbs, LED strips, overhead fluorescent tubes — are spectrally narrow, directionally concentrated, or color-shifted in ways that misrepresent item color. They also frequently produce mixed color temperatures when combined with ambient daylight, creating the half-orange, half-blue color splits that are immediately recognizable as amateur product photography.

The exception is purpose-built photography lighting: softboxes, ring lights with daylight-balanced LEDs, and lightboxes designed for product photography. These can match or exceed natural light quality when used correctly.

## Identifying Bad Lighting by Type

Harsh single-direction shadows are produced by a single light source positioned to one side without diffusion. The symptom is a hard shadow bisecting the item with a sharp edge, typically covering 30 to 60 percent of the visible surface. Detail in the shadow zone is lost. The item appears to have two distinct zones: a bright, potentially over-exposed side and a dark, detail-free side.

Yellow or orange color cast indicates a tungsten or warm LED light source without color correction. Wood, fabric, and leather appear richer than their actual color. White ceramic, silver metal, and light-colored cloth appear distinctly yellow. Buyers who receive items expecting the warm tones of the photo and find a cooler, more neutral item in person feel deceived even if the seller described the item accurately.

Blue or cool color cast indicates an overcast daylight source without white balance correction, or a cool LED source. This is less common than yellow cast but produces the opposite problem — items that appear colder and less appealing in photos than in person.

Mixed color temperature is one of the most immediately damaging lighting problems. It occurs when two light sources of different color temperatures illuminate the item simultaneously — most commonly a window providing daylight on one side and a lamp providing tungsten light on the other. The result is an item that is half blue and half orange, with no area that accurately represents actual color.

Lens flare appears as geometric shapes or streaks of light overlaid on the image, typically caused by shooting with a light source in or near the camera's field of view. It reduces contrast, obscures surface detail, and marks the photo as taken by an inexperienced photographer.

Backlighting occurs when the primary light source is behind the item from the camera's perspective. The item becomes a silhouette with a bright background. Surface detail, color, and texture are all destroyed. This is the lighting configuration produced by placing an item in front of a window and photographing it without compensating exposure.

Underexposure without lighting problems is distinct from poor lighting: the camera did not receive enough light, and the entire image is dark. Digital brightening of underexposed photos reveals noise and color artifacts and should not be relied on as a substitute for adequate lighting.

## Shadow Analysis

Ideal shadows in product photography are soft — meaning the transition from lit area to shadow area is gradual, not a sharp line — and they fall away from the camera rather than toward it.

A shadow that falls toward the camera obscures the item's surface. A shadow that falls away from the camera creates the illusion of depth and three-dimensionality without concealing surface detail.

For most items, the ideal shadow fills no more than 15 to 20 percent of the item's visible surface. Shadows on the background are generally acceptable and can suggest professional studio photography when they are soft and positioned correctly.

Evaluate shadows by asking: Does the shadow on the item obscure any feature a buyer would want to inspect? If yes, the lighting requires remediation.

## Lighting Score: 1 to 10

A score of 10 means even, diffused lighting with no discernible color cast, soft shadows on the background only, accurate color rendering across all materials, and no blown highlights or blocked-up shadows. Professional studio quality.

A score of 9 means near-perfect lighting with one minor issue — a slight shadow on a secondary surface, or a marginally warm color balance that does not materially misrepresent color.

A score of 7 to 8 means good quality natural lighting with some shadow on the item, or artificial lighting that is reasonably balanced and diffused. Acceptable for listing at any price point.

A score of 5 to 6 means a single lighting problem — a noticeable color cast, a shadow covering one area, or moderate backlight — that reduces photo quality but does not completely destroy the image's utility.

A score of 3 to 4 means multiple lighting problems or a single severe problem — harsh directional shadow splitting the item, strong color cast that misrepresents material color, or significant backlighting. These photos should be retaken.

A score of 1 to 2 means the lighting makes the item effectively unviewable — silhouetted against a window, photographed in near-dark conditions, or with such severe color distortion that the item cannot be identified.

## What Lighting Communicates About the Seller

Buyers unconsciously attribute effort and care in photography to the same seller behaviors applied to the item itself. A seller who carefully lit their photographs probably stored the item carefully. A seller who photographed a $200 item under a yellow kitchen ceiling lamp with deep shadows probably did not give the item much thought.

This is not always accurate — excellent photographers may be indifferent owners, and meticulous owners may be poor photographers — but it is the buyer's automatic inference, and it affects bidding behavior whether or not either party is aware of it.

For high-value items, the quality of lighting in photographs is itself a value signal. Auction houses use controlled studio lighting for a reason: the photography communicates that the house takes the item seriously.

## Remediation Recommendations

For yellow or orange cast: recommend photographing near a north-facing or east-facing window during daylight hours with no lamps on in the room. If a lightbox is available, use daylight-balanced LED strips.

For shadows splitting the item: recommend moving the item closer to the window, turning the item so the window light falls on the primary face, or using a white foam board on the shadow side as a reflector.

For backlighting from a window: recommend turning the item so the window is beside or behind the camera, not behind the item.

For mixed color temperature: recommend turning off all lamps in the room and photographing with window light only, or turning off all windows (curtains closed) and photographing with consistent artificial lighting.

For underexposure: recommend moving to a brighter location or increasing the ambient light before photographing. Do not recommend post-processing brightness adjustments as a primary solution.

For direct harsh sunlight: recommend moving to open shade — a location where the sky is visible but the sun is not — or photographing during the golden hour within 30 minutes of sunrise or sunset.
