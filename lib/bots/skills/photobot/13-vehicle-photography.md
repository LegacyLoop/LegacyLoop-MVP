# Vehicle Photography

## The 14-Angle Vehicle Coverage Map

A complete vehicle photo set consists of 14 specific angles and subjects. Each angle serves a
distinct purpose in the buyer's evaluation process. Omitting any angle from this set introduces
an information gap that sophisticated buyers — especially those buying sight-unseen — will flag
as a reason to either ask for more photos or abandon the listing.

### Angle 1: Front Three-Quarter (Hero)

The front three-quarter view is the hero photo for vehicle listings. Shot from approximately 45
degrees off the front corner (driver side is traditional, passenger side for right-hand-drive
markets), this angle shows the most character of any vehicle — it reveals the front fascia, the
roofline profile, the wheel design, and the body length in a single frame. This is the photo that
determines click-through in search results. It must be shot in flattering light with the vehicle
clean and on a visually clean background.

### Angle 2: Rear Three-Quarter

The rear three-quarter from the opposite corner from the hero. Shows the trunk line, rear lights,
rear wheel, and body proportions. Buyers compare front and rear three-quarter views as the primary
pair for assessing the overall body condition and style.

### Angle 3: Driver Side Profile

Full side view from sufficient distance to show the entire vehicle without perspective distortion.
The vehicle should fill the frame from bumper to bumper with sky or a clean background above the
roofline. This angle reveals straightness of the body panels — any wave, crease, or misalignment
in the body is most visible in a true profile shot.

### Angle 4: Passenger Side Profile

Same as driver side, from the opposite side. Both profile shots are required because damage,
repairs, and panel replacements are often confined to one side of the vehicle.

### Angle 5: Front Straight-On

Direct front view, camera level with the center of the grille. Shows front fascia condition,
headlight symmetry, grille, bumper, and any front-end damage or repairs. Sophisticated buyers
look for color match discrepancies between the hood and fenders in this view, which indicate
previous body work.

### Angle 6: Rear Straight-On

Direct rear view. Shows trunk lid, rear bumper, tail lights, badge condition, tow hitch if
present, and exhaust tips. Exhaust tip coloration (black soot suggests oil burning, white residue
is normal) gives mechanically knowledgeable buyers information about engine condition.

### Angle 7: Engine Bay

Open hood, camera positioned to show the complete engine bay from the front. This photo
communicates mechanical care more directly than any other exterior shot. A clean engine bay
signals an owner who maintained the vehicle. Oil sludge, coolant residue, cracked hoses, and
corrosion are visible in this photo and must be shown honestly. Buyers making high-price decisions
on sight-unseen vehicles treat the engine bay photo as the single most important trust signal.

### Angle 8: Interior Dashboard From Driver Seat

Seated position in the driver's seat, or camera positioned at driver's seat height, capturing the
full dashboard, steering wheel, gauge cluster, center stack, and infotainment screen. All screens
should be shown powered on where possible. The odometer reading should be partially visible or
clearly readable. Climate controls, radio, and any aftermarket additions should be visible.

### Angle 9: Interior Rear Seat

Shows the rear seat condition including fabric or leather wear, head restraints, center armrest
if present, seatbelts, and rear door panels. For three-row vehicles, a separate third-row photo
is expected.

### Angle 10: Odometer Close-Up

A dedicated close-up photo of the odometer showing the current mileage reading. This is the
verification anchor for the listing. No vehicle listing should be published without a clear,
readable odometer photo. Platforms that specialize in vehicle sales treat a missing odometer photo
as a red flag.

### Angle 11: VIN Plate Close-Up

The Vehicle Identification Number plate, visible through the driver's side windshield on the
dashboard, or on the door jamb sticker. Both locations can be photographed. The VIN is the
verification anchor for ownership, vehicle history, and specification confirmation. Buyers
conducting vehicle history checks cross-reference the listed VIN against the physical plate.

### Angle 12: Tire Tread Depth

At least one tire, showing the tread depth with a visible penny or quarter test. The penny test
is standard: insert a US penny into the tread groove with Lincoln's head facing down. If the top
of Lincoln's head is visible, the tire is at or below the 2/32 inch replacement threshold.
If Lincoln's forehead is obscured, tread depth is acceptable. Show the test in the photo.

### Angle 13: Undercarriage

If safely accessible, photograph the undercarriage showing the frame, exhaust system, suspension
components, and any rust or repair evidence. This photo is particularly important for vehicles
in rust-belt markets or coastal regions where salt exposure is a primary concern. Bring a
Bring a Trailer and Cars and Bids buyers in particular look for undercarriage photos as a
standard part of their pre-bid evaluation.

### Angle 14: Trunk or Cargo Area

Open trunk or cargo area showing interior lining condition, spare tire or tire kit presence,
and overall cleanliness. For wagons, SUVs, and trucks: photograph cargo area in standard
configuration and, if applicable, with seats folded.

## Why Each Angle Matters to the Buyer

Front three-quarter is the hero because it shows the most in a single frame. Engine bay is the
mechanical trust photo. Odometer and VIN are the documentation anchors. Tire tread is the safety
and maintenance disclosure photo. The undercarriage is the rust and structural integrity photo.

Buyers bidding or purchasing sight-unseen conduct a complete mental inspection using these 14
photos. A missing angle creates a gap they fill with a negative assumption. When the engine bay
photo is missing, buyers assume the engine bay is dirty or damaged. When the undercarriage is
missing, buyers assume rust. Completeness is not just courtesy — it is active sale management.

## Condition Documentation: Show Every Flaw

For every dent, scratch, paint chip, rust spot, interior stain, torn seat, cracked plastic trim,
or chipped glass — photograph it. Add a close-up photo of the defect to the set. This is not
optional for high-price vehicle listings.

The documentation principle: disclose everything in photos before the sale. A buyer who discovers
an undisclosed defect at delivery has leverage for a price dispute, partial refund, or return.
A buyer who sees every defect in the listing photos has pre-accepted those defects as part of
their purchase decision. Honest photo disclosure protects both parties.

## Walk-Around Video

Video is increasingly expected in the premium vehicle resale market. Bring a Trailer and Cars
and Bids both encourage or require video as part of listing submissions. A simple walk-around
video — 60 to 90 seconds, continuous take, starting at the front and moving clockwise around
the vehicle at a steady pace — provides buyers with continuity evidence that individual photos
cannot. Video makes it significantly harder to stage or hide localized damage.

Interior video is also valuable: a 30-second sweep of the interior starting at the driver seat,
panning to the dashboard, center console, rear seat, and cargo area gives buyers a continuous
view that still photography cannot replicate.

## Time of Day: The Golden Hour Rule

Vehicle paint is most accurately and attractively photographed in early morning or late afternoon
light — the period roughly one hour after sunrise and one hour before sunset. This is called
golden hour. The sun angle during golden hour creates warm, directional light that enhances paint
depth and metallic flake without creating the harsh midday shadows that bisect body panels and
obscure panel condition.

Overcast days are the second-best option for vehicles. Overcast cloud cover acts as a giant
natural softbox, producing even, shadow-free illumination that shows body panel condition clearly.
Avoid photographing vehicles in direct midday sun: the shadows are harsh, the paint looks flat,
and metallic finishes bloom into hot spots that obscure the true paint color.

## Auction Platform Photo Standards

Barrett-Jackson professional consignment: minimum 30 high-quality photos, covering all 14 standard
angles plus additional detail shots of any special equipment, documentation, restoration work,
badges, and serialized components.

Bring a Trailer: user-submitted photos, but the BaT community comments will publicly flag missing
angles. BaT listings with complete 14-angle coverage receive materially more bids than incomplete
submissions. BaT explicitly recommends undercarriage photos and criticizes their absence.

Cars and Bids: similar to BaT in community expectations. Listings without engine bay, odometer,
and undercarriage photos are routinely noted in buyer comments as incomplete, which suppresses
competitive bidding.

Private sale on general platforms (Facebook, Craigslist, Autotrader): the 14-angle standard
remains the correct target, but buyers in this market are somewhat less demanding than auction
specialists. Hero, engine bay, interior, odometer, and VIN are the minimum acceptable set.
