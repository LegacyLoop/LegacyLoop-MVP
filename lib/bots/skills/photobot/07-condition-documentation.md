# Condition Documentation

## The Iron Rule

Never hide, minimize, or obscure damage in a product listing.

This rule is not primarily ethical, though it is that. It is financial. Sellers who document condition honestly — including every scratch, chip, stain, crack, repair, and missing part — sell for more money, receive fewer return requests, accumulate better feedback ratings, and build buyer relationships that produce repeat sales.

The mechanism is buyer psychology. A buyer who discovers a flaw during their own inspection — after the item has arrived, after the excitement of the purchase has worn off, after they have committed financially — feels deceived even if the flaw was visible in the photos but not called out explicitly. That buyer files a return request, leaves negative feedback, and tells other buyers about their experience.

A buyer who finds the same flaw documented in the listing photographs before the purchase makes an informed decision to buy at the listed price, accepting the condition as described. If anything, that buyer tends to be more satisfied — the item matches their expectations exactly, and the seller's transparency has pre-built trust.

Honest condition documentation transforms potential complaints into pre-accepted facts.

## Why Transparent Flaw Documentation Increases Sale Price

This counterintuitive outcome is documented across professional resale categories and follows from two mechanisms.

First, comprehensive flaw documentation signals expertise and honesty. A seller who has photographed every minor scratch and noted every cosmetic imperfection is clearly a seller who has examined the item carefully. Buyers extend the inference that the seller knows the item's true condition and has not missed anything material. This reduces the buyer's risk perception, and lower perceived risk supports higher bids and faster offers.

Second, thoroughly documented items attract confident buyers. The subset of buyers who understand item values and are willing to pay fair market prices are experienced buyers who need complete condition information to make their decision. Incomplete condition documentation deters experienced buyers and attracts only inexperienced buyers who make low offers.

A $500 item with four flaw photos that document every imperfection will attract knowledgeable buyers willing to pay $450 to $500. The same item with no flaw photos will attract cautious buyers willing to pay $250 to $350. The honest documentation added $100 to $200 to the final sale price.

## How to Show Wear with Dignity

Flaw photography requires judgment. The goal is accurate representation, not maximally unflattering presentation.

Document flaws in context before documenting them in isolation. Show the full item, then show the area of the flaw in a medium shot, then show the flaw in close-up. This three-step sequence gives the buyer both the context — how large is the flaw relative to the item? — and the detail — what exactly is the flaw?

Use neutral, even lighting for flaw documentation. Harsh raking light from a single side exaggerates texture, scratches, and surface irregularities far beyond their actual visual impact. This is counterproductive: the goal is accurate representation, and raking light misrepresents condition as much as flattering light does. A softly diffused light source at roughly 45 degrees to the surface gives the best balance of shadow definition (which makes scratches visible) without exaggeration.

Photograph from the angle that accurately represents the flaw. Not the worst angle, and not the best angle — the honest angle. For a scratch on a glossy surface, this is the angle at which the scratch is clearly visible in person. For a chip on a rim, this is the straight-on angle at the chip's level. Photographing a chip from above to minimize its appearance, or from directly in front with a harsh light to maximize it, both misrepresent the item.

Include a measurement reference for any flaw larger than a pinpoint. A coin, a ruler, a standard postage stamp, or any recognizable reference object placed beside the flaw gives the buyer an accurate sense of scale. A 3mm chip is minor. A 3cm chip is major. Without a scale reference, the buyer cannot assess which they are looking at.

## What Buyers Expect to See

An experienced resale buyer approaches a listing with a mental checklist of condition concerns specific to the item category. When the listing does not address those concerns with photographs, the buyer assumes the problems exist and have been concealed.

For all items: every visible scratch, dent, chip, crack, stain, discoloration, fade, repair, replaced part, missing part, or structural damage must appear in at least one photograph.

For items described as restored, refinished, repaired, or professionally cleaned: the specific areas of restoration must be documented even if they look good. Buyers who value all-original items need to know what has been altered.

For items described as complete with original packaging: the packaging must be photographed separately showing its own condition, including any damage to the box, case, or accessories.

For items described as working or tested: this cannot be verified by photography but should be noted in the listing, and any visible wear related to use — on buttons, switches, knobs, and ports — should be documented.

## Category-Specific Condition Documentation

Furniture: photograph all four legs from a knee-height angle showing any wobble repairs, replaced feet, or cracks. Photograph all drawer interiors showing tracks, slides, and any drawer bottom repairs. Photograph all joints and connections showing any separation, glue residue, or repairs. Photograph all hardware — hinges, pulls, locks — showing any replacement, corrosion, or damage. Photograph the finish at multiple points showing scratches, watermarks, white rings, loss, crazing, or any refinishing.

Silver: photograph all dents showing recto and verso views. Photograph any monogram with sufficient scale to show letter identification. Photograph all hallmark sequences. Photograph any repairs to solder joints, splits, or replaced components. Photograph the overall piece on a clean dark background to show any pitting, tarnish patterns, or restoration areas.

Porcelain and ceramics: photograph all rim sections in sequence, moving around the piece to show any chips, hairlines, or restoration. Hold the piece up to a light source and photograph any hairline cracks that are only visible with transmitted light. Photograph the base showing any chips, kiln marks, or previous repairs. Photograph any visible restoration work under a UV light if restoration fluorescence is evident.

Jewelry: photograph the clasp mechanism showing any wear, replacement, or damage. Photograph all stone settings showing any loose prongs, missing stones, or chips. Photograph the backs of all stones to document chips visible from the reverse. Photograph any solder repairs or sizing marks on ring shanks.

Electronics: photograph the screen showing any dead pixels, scratches, or cracks with the display active. Photograph all ports showing corrosion, bent pins, or physical damage. Photograph all physical controls showing any fading, sticking, or missing labels. Photograph the battery compartment showing contact condition and any corrosion.

Clothing: photograph any seam stress or separation points. Photograph any stains against a white background with bright light showing size and color. Photograph any pilling on fabric surfaces. Photograph all closures — zippers, buttons, snaps — showing any damage or replacement. Photograph any fading or color inconsistency in natural light.

## The Return-Proof Photo Set

The standard for condition documentation in any listing above $100 in value is what professional auction house cataloguers call the return-proof photo set: a collection of condition photographs so thorough that the buyer cannot claim any damage as a surprise.

If a buyer could theoretically receive the item and find something they did not expect to find, the condition documentation is incomplete. This is the test.

Review the complete photo set and ask: is there any area of the item that a buyer has not seen? Is there any visible imperfection that has not been shown in at least one photo? If the answer to either question is yes, additional photos are needed before the item is listed.

## Number of Condition Photos by Price Tier

For items priced at $50 or under: one to two condition photos are sufficient. One photo showing the overall item in good light to demonstrate condition, plus one close-up of the primary area of wear if any exists.

For items priced at $51 to $200: two to four condition photos. Document all visible flaws individually. If the item is in excellent condition with no flaws, two photos from different angles under good lighting demonstrating the absence of wear are sufficient.

For items priced at $201 to $1,000: four to eight condition photos. Each distinct flaw should have its own photograph. Areas of common concern for the category — rims, legs, joints, surfaces — should each be documented even if no flaw is present, because the absence of a photo in a high-concern area is interpreted as concealment by experienced buyers at this price level.

For items priced above $1,000: every single visible imperfection must be individually photographed and captioned with its location on the item. The condition photo set at this price tier should be exhaustive. Auction houses routinely include fifteen to twenty-five condition photographs for items above $5,000. No buyer at this price level will make an offer without comprehensive documentation, and any buyer who does has already discounted the purchase price to account for unknown condition risks.
