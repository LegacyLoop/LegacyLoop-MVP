# Angle Coverage Standards

## Why Angle Coverage Determines Buyer Confidence

Every angle that is missing from a listing is a question the buyer cannot answer. Buyers who have questions they cannot answer do not make offers. They move on to the next listing, where another seller has answered those questions with photographs.

The absence of an angle also carries a secondary message: the seller may be concealing something. A furniture listing with no photo of the underside, no photo of the back panel, and no photo of the drawer interiors signals — whether intentionally or not — that there are problems in those areas the seller would prefer the buyer not examine. This is not a rational inference on the buyer's part. It is an automatic pattern-recognition response built by experience with deceptive listings.

Complete angle coverage removes the ambiguity. A buyer who has seen every required angle for an item category can make a purchase decision with confidence, and confident buyers pay more and return more often.

## General Items: 8 Required Angles

For items that do not fit a specialized category — household goods, tools, kitchenware, accessories, decor — a complete listing requires the following eight angles.

Front: the primary face of the item as it would appear displayed or in use. This is always the first angle documented.

Back: the full reverse of the item. Many items have labels, serial numbers, or structural information on the back that buyers need.

Left side: a true 90-degree side view from the item's left.

Right side: a true 90-degree side view from the item's right.

Top or overhead: looking straight down at the item from above. Essential for dishes, trays, boxes, and any item where the top surface is a primary feature.

Detail: a close-up of the most important or distinctive feature of the item — a clasp, a carving, a logo, a texture.

Maker mark: any signature, stamp, label, patent number, or manufacturer mark photographed in isolation with maximum sharpness.

Condition or flaw: every area of wear, damage, repair, or imperfection documented in its own photograph. If there are no flaws, a photo showing the clearest, most undamaged surface area with sufficient lighting to prove the absence of flaws.

## Antiques: 11 Required Angles

Antiques require the 8 general angles plus the following three additional angles.

Bottom or underside: the base of the item. Pottery, silver, bronze, and furniture all carry identifying marks, construction details, and condition information on their undersides that significantly affect value and authenticity.

Joinery or construction detail: a close-up showing how the item was made — dovetail joints, hand-cut mortise and tenon, hand-hammered metal, thrown pottery, blown glass. This is evidence of age and manufacture method that cannot be assessed from the face of the item.

Provenance label: any auction sticker, estate label, collection stamp, appraisal sticker, or exhibition label on the item. These can double or triple an item's value and are the first thing a serious collector looks for.

## Collectibles: 9 Required Angles

Collectibles require the 8 general angles plus the following additional angles.

Corners: all four corners documented individually. Corners take the most wear on books, cards, boxes, tins, and printed collectibles. Buyers of collectibles grade corners and will not bid without seeing them.

Surface close-up: a macro shot of the primary surface showing texture, print quality, and surface condition at maximum resolution.

Centering reference if applicable: for cards, stamps, and printed items, a photo showing how centered the image is within the borders. Off-center items command significantly lower prices in graded collectible markets.

Packaging or original materials if present: the original box, certificate of authenticity, hang tag, dust jacket, or case. These multiply value substantially and must be documented separately.

Authentication marks if present: grading service labels, authentication holograms, stamps, or certificates photographed clearly.

## Vehicles: 14 Required Angles

Vehicles require a more extensive angle set due to the scale of the purchase decision and the number of inspectable systems.

Exterior front three-quarter: the most common hero angle for vehicles, showing the front and one side simultaneously.

Exterior rear three-quarter: matching the front three-quarter from the opposite corner.

Driver side profile: a true side elevation showing the full length of the vehicle.

Passenger side profile: the opposite side elevation.

Engine bay with hood open: a full view of the engine compartment, clearly lit, showing all major components.

Interior dashboard: the full instrument cluster and center console from the driver seat.

Rear seat area: the full rear passenger compartment showing headroom, condition of upholstery, and floor.

Odometer: the current mileage reading clearly legible. This is legally significant and non-negotiable.

VIN plate: the visible VIN on the dashboard or door jamb, clearly legible.

Tire tread: at least one tire, ideally all four, showing tread depth. A coin or tread depth gauge in the photo provides measurable reference.

Undercarriage: the underside of the vehicle showing frame condition, exhaust, and any visible rust or damage. This is often the most neglected and most important angle for used vehicle transactions.

Trunk or cargo area: the full interior with floor and walls visible.

Any damage area: every dent, scratch, rust spot, or trim deficiency photographed individually in close-up.

Any recent repair documentation: photos of receipts, service records, or visible repairs if present.

## The Angle Coverage Score

The angle coverage score is calculated as the number of required angles documented divided by the total number of required angles for the item's category, multiplied by 100.

A score of 100 means all required angles are present and the listing is complete from a coverage standpoint.

A score of 80 to 99 means one or two angles are missing. The listing is acceptable for lower-value items but should be completed before listing items above $100.

A score of 60 to 79 means multiple angles are missing. The buyer is left with significant unresolved questions. These listings produce fewer offers and lower final prices across all categories.

A score below 60 means the listing is fundamentally incomplete. It should not be published until the missing angles are provided.

## Priority Ranking of Missing Angles by Buyer Impact

When only one or two shots can be added in a reshoot, prioritize in this order.

First priority: maker marks and provenance labels. These have the highest impact on perceived value and authenticity.

Second priority: condition and flaw documentation. Missing flaw photos are the primary driver of return requests and negative feedback.

Third priority: underside and construction detail for antiques. Serious buyers will not make offers without these.

Fourth priority: back panel for all items. Serial numbers, labels, and structural information live here.

Fifth priority: side views. These establish three-dimensionality and proportions that buyers need to assess fit and scale.

Sixth priority: detail close-ups. These help with identification and increase perceived quality even when the item is fully visible in other shots.
