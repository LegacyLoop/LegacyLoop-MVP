# High-Value Item Standards

## The Threshold: When Photo Standards Change

For items valued below $500, a quality phone photo set following basic technique guidelines is
fully adequate for online resale. Above $500, buyer behavior changes measurably. Buyers making a
$500 or $1,000 or $5,000 purchase scrutinize photos at a level that casual buyers do not. They
zoom in on surface details. They look for signs of undisclosed damage. They use photo quality as
a proxy for seller trustworthiness. At this price tier, photo quality is not cosmetic — it directly
affects sale probability and achievable sale price.

The practical threshold for professional photography consideration is approximately $2,000. Below
that figure, a skilled phone photo set with good natural light and complete angle coverage can
compete. Above it, professional photography pays for itself.

## Professional Photography Investment and ROI

The return on investment calculation for professional photography of high-value items is
straightforward and favorable.

A professional product photography session runs between $150 and $400 for a single item, depending
on market and photographer. For a $5,000 antique, research consistently shows that professional
photography can increase achieved sale price by 15 to 25 percent — a gain of $750 to $1,250 on a
$5,000 item, against an investment of $200 to $400.

The mechanism is direct: better photos generate more bids at auction, more serious buyer inquiries
on direct-sale platforms, and stronger starting prices for negotiation. Collectors and dealers
who regularly buy at this price point have trained eyes for photo quality. They associate
professional photos with professional provenance management, honest condition disclosure, and
overall seller reliability.

For auction house consignment specifically, professional photos are often required, and the auction
house may provide photography services as part of their consignment fee. When submitting to auction
houses as an independent consignor, professional photos submitted with the initial inquiry almost
always result in faster responses and better estimated lot reserves.

## When to Recommend Professional Photography Services

Recommend professional photography when any of the following conditions apply:

The item has a known or suspected value above $2,000 and has auction house potential. Auction
house estimates for photography-supported consignments are materially higher than phone-photo
submissions.

The item has maker marks, signatures, hallmarks, or stamps that require macro detail photography
at resolutions that phone cameras cannot consistently achieve. Fine maker marks on silver, faint
signatures on paintings, small cartouches on ceramics, and die-struck hallmarks on jewelry all
require macro photography at 1:1 magnification or better.

The item is in a category where condition detail drives price: jewelry, watches, vintage cameras,
fine art, antique maps, rare books, coins, and musical instruments. In these categories, a single
undocumented scratch or undisclosed repair at the $5,000 price point can result in a dispute and
full refund. Professional macro photography documents condition honestly and completely.

The item is part of a collection or estate where multiple high-value items will be sold. A single
photography session for 8 to 12 items amortizes the session cost and creates a consistent
professional visual presentation across the collection.

## What a Professional Product Photo Set Includes

A competent professional estate or antique photography session delivers the following:

Tethered shooting with instant review on a calibrated monitor, allowing the photographer and
client to review each photo before moving to the next angle and catch any issues — focus errors,
incorrect exposure, missing details — immediately.

Calibrated continuous lighting (softboxes or LED panels) that eliminates harsh shadows, ensures
even illumination across the item's surface, and reveals surface texture, glaze, patina, and
material character accurately.

A color checker card photographed in the same lighting conditions as the item. This card is used
in post-processing to calibrate color accuracy, ensuring that the blues, reds, and yellows in the
final photos match the actual item under daylight conditions. This is particularly critical for
textiles, ceramics, paintings, and any item where color is a material attribute of value.

High-resolution output files: minimum 3000 pixels on the longest side, delivered as uncompressed
TIFF or high-quality JPEG, suitable for catalog printing and digital publication.

A complete angle set: front, back, both sides, top-down, detail close-ups, maker marks at macro,
scale reference, and condition documentation of any wear or repairs. For furniture: joint details,
underside of table surfaces, foot condition, interior of drawers, hardware close-ups.

Consistency across the set: same lighting, same background, same distance and perspective rules
applied to every angle, so the photo set reads as a unified professional document rather than
a collection of individual snapshots.

## The Sotheby's Standard

The operational benchmark for high-value estate photography is the photo set that meets Sotheby's
catalog submission requirements. Understanding this standard defines the ceiling toward which
high-value estate photography aspires, and clarifies the floor for serious auction consignment.

Sotheby's expects 12 or more photos per lot for significant items. A single decorative arts piece
of meaningful value will typically have: primary hero image (item fills 70 percent of the frame
on white background), secondary view from opposite angle, both side profiles, top-down, underside
or back, maker's mark macro, condition documentation (up to 3 or 4 photos showing all material
defects), scale reference, and any accompanying provenance documentation photographed flat under
even lighting.

Color accuracy is non-negotiable at the Sotheby's standard. A photo that misrepresents the color
of a Chinese export porcelain vase is not merely a bad photo — it is a material misrepresentation
that creates legal exposure and damages the auction house's credibility.

The 70 percent frame rule: the item should fill approximately 70 percent of the frame in the hero
image. This is a composition standard that balances showing the complete item against allowing the
eye to see its full form without the frame feeling cramped.

## When Phone Photos Are Acceptable for High-Value Items

Professional photography is recommended but not always possible. Phone photos are acceptable for
high-value items in the following circumstances:

The item is too large to transport to a photography studio — a large grandfather clock, an oversized
tapestry, a heavy piece of garden statuary. In these cases, the goal is to replicate as many
professional photography principles as possible on location: consistent lighting from a window or
supplemental LED panel, clean backdrop, steady camera on a tripod or phone stand, and full angle
coverage.

The seller is remote, rural, or physically unable to arrange professional photography. In this case,
a detailed, technically correct phone photo set with a complete angle map is the best available
option and should be executed with as much care and completeness as possible.

The sale is time-sensitive — an active estate liquidation, a pending house sale, a court-ordered
estate sale with a fixed deadline. Time pressure sometimes requires accepting the photo quality
possible within the available time rather than waiting for a professional session.

In all of these cases, the quality of the phone photo set becomes more important, not less.
Careful setup, controlled lighting, complete angle coverage, and macro documentation of all
identifying marks and condition issues compensate meaningfully for the absence of professional
studio equipment.
