## The 12-Dimension Forensic Scoring Rubric

MEGA_PHOTO defines a 12-field scoring panel. Each field is scored
1-10 with a specific justification. This section defines the
methodology the 4-AI panel uses to arrive at each score honestly.

### 1. overallPhotoScore (1-10)

The composite score. Not an average — a weighted synthesis that
reflects the photo set's total fitness for listing use.

- 10: Tack-sharp, pro lighting, full angle coverage, scale
  reference present, all condition issues documented, platform-
  optimized composition. Ready to publish without edits.
- 8-9: Strong overall with 1-2 minor issues (soft backdrop,
  missing one non-critical angle, slight exposure variance).
- 6-7: Functional but visibly amateur. Will sell, but leaves
  price on the table.
- 4-5: Multiple problems — inconsistent lighting, missing
  angles, or clutter. Needs significant re-shoot.
- 1-3: Listing-blocking. Blur, wrong item visible, obstructed
  view, single-angle only on multi-angle category.

### 2. presentationScore (1-10)

How professional does this look to a buyer scrolling a feed at
10 items per second? Presentation is the first-impression layer
— does the photo stop the scroll or blend into noise?

- 10: Studio-grade isolation, perfect exposure, magazine-level
  styling. Scroll-stopper.
- 7-9: Clean home-studio quality. Obvious care taken.
- 4-6: Amateur but organized. Clean backdrop attempt, reasonable
  framing.
- 1-3: Cluttered environment, distracting elements, poor
  framing. Reads as "didn't bother."

### 3. backgroundScore (1-10)

Cleanliness, neutrality, and distraction-free composition.

- 10: Seamless white or neutral gray, zero distraction.
- 7-9: Clean surface (hardwood floor, neutral wall, linen
  backdrop) with minor imperfection.
- 4-6: Home environment visible but not chaotic (couch in
  background, lamp edge).
- 1-3: Kitchen clutter, bed linens, toys, pets, other items
  in frame.

### 4. lightingScore (1-10)

Evenness, accuracy, shadow control.

- 10: Three-point or window+reflector setup, zero hotspots,
  gentle shadows shaping form.
- 7-9: Single soft source handled well (diffused window,
  bounced flash).
- 4-6: Visible hotspots or harsh shadows. Item still
  recognizable but unflattering.
- 1-3: Flash glare, deep shadows obscuring detail, mixed
  color temperature (tungsten + daylight).

### 5. compositionScore (1-10)

Framing, centering, rule-of-thirds.

- 10: Intentional composition — rule-of-thirds placement,
  negative space used, item size appropriate to frame.
- 7-9: Centered and well-framed with minor awkwardness.
- 4-6: Off-center in an unintentional way, or item crowded
  to edge.
- 1-3: Item cut off, tilted horizon, unusable framing.

### 6. colorFidelity (1-10)

How true-to-life are the colors the buyer will see?

- 10: White balance accurate, colors vibrant but not pushed.
- 7-9: Minor color cast (slight warm from incandescent) but
  corrigible.
- 4-6: Noticeable cast — item's actual color is hard to read.
- 1-3: Heavy filter or extreme cast that misrepresents the
  item (yellow guitar photographed green).

### 7. detailCapture (1-10)

Can buyers see texture, markings, condition?

- 10: Macro-level detail where it matters (serial numbers
  readable, grain visible, stitching crisp).
- 7-9: Standard detail sufficient for the category.
- 4-6: Detail missing at critical spots (label hidden, mark
  blurred).
- 1-3: Everything is soft — buyer cannot verify anything.

### 8. sharpnessScore (1-10)

Is the item tack-sharp? Any motion blur or focus issues?

- 10: Pixel-perfect sharpness on primary subject.
- 7-9: Sharp on key plane with acceptable depth.
- 4-6: Soft but usable — buyer won't reject outright.
- 1-3: Motion blur, out of focus, camera shake visible.

### 9. exposureAccuracy (1-10)

Proper exposure? Blown highlights or crushed shadows?

- 10: Full tonal range preserved — highlights just shy of
  clipping, shadows readable.
- 7-9: Minor exposure drift but item readable.
- 4-6: Silhouette or washed-out — item visible but tonally
  flat or extreme.
- 1-3: Detail lost entirely in blown whites or black shadows.

### 10. scaleClarity (1-10)

Can the buyer tell the actual size? Is there a scale reference?

- 10: Explicit scale reference present (ruler, coin, hand,
  known object) in at least one photo.
- 7-9: Size implied by context (chair next to known-size
  lamp).
- 4-6: No scale reference, but category makes size obvious
  (wristwatch).
- 1-3: Impossible to tell — detail shot with no context, or
  item photographed in void.

### 11. emotionalAppeal (1-10)

Does this photo make a buyer want to own this item? Does it
convey value and desirability?

- 10: Photo tells a story — the item looks like something
  worth owning.
- 7-9: Clean and attractive without narrative.
- 4-6: Functional but uninspiring.
- 1-3: Actively unappealing — dirty-looking, sad, or
  institutional (looks like a pawn-shop photo).

### 12. mobileRendering (1-10)

Will this photo look good on a 6-inch phone screen? Is
text/detail readable at mobile scale?

- 10: Primary subject readable at thumbnail size, hero
  element occupies majority of frame.
- 7-9: Clear at phone scale, some detail may require zoom.
- 4-6: Item looks small or cluttered at phone scale — buyer
  scrolls past.
- 1-3: Unreadable on mobile — too much background, too
  small a subject.

The composite score is the basis of overallPhotoScore. Never
output scores without per-field justification in the reasoning
text.

## The IRON RULE — Condition Authenticity Preservation

MEGA_PHOTO lines 381-389 define the single most important rule in
PhotoBot MegaBot operation. It is restated here because it governs
every variation, every DALL-E prompt, every edit recommendation.

**NEVER suggest hiding, minimizing, softening, retouching, or
altering ANY condition detail:**

- Scratches, dents, chips, cracks, scuffs, wear marks
- Water damage, sun fading, stains, discoloration
- Patina, tarnish, oxidation, rust, corrosion
- Missing parts, repairs, alterations, replaced components
- Fraying, peeling, warping, yellowing

Every condition issue MUST remain visible in every generated
image and variation. Presentation means professional — clean
backdrop, even lighting, honest framing. Presentation does NOT
mean dishonest — hidden chips, softened scratches, cloned-out
stains. That crosses a bright line that destroys buyer trust
and violates platform policies.

### Why the iron rule matters

Every "Condition Celebration" variation that makes patina look
beautiful is legitimate. Every "Auction Catalog" variation that
shows condition honestly under professional lighting is
legitimate. What is NOT legitimate is any instruction that tells
DALL-E to remove, minimize, or obscure a documented condition
issue.

The distinction is: make the item look like itself, honestly,
well-lit and well-framed. Not: make the item look better than
it is.

## Lighting Assessment

Four specialist-level lighting signals inform lightingScore:

### Natural vs artificial

- Natural (window light, diffused): generally forgiving but
  time-of-day dependent. Golden hour produces warm cast; noon
  produces hard shadows if not diffused.
- Artificial continuous (LED panels, work lights): consistent
  but color-temperature-sensitive. Mixed sources create cast.
- Strobe/flash: powerful but harsh without diffusion.
- Mixed sources: worst case — tungsten lamp + window light =
  dual color temperature that no single white-balance setting
  fixes.

### Hotspot detection

A hotspot is a blown-out bright region where detail is lost
entirely (pure white pixels). Common causes: direct flash on
glossy surface, single bare bulb, unfiltered window sun.

### Color cast

A cast is a uniform tint across the image. Common casts:
- Warm orange: incandescent bulbs, golden hour
- Cool blue: open shade, cloudy daylight
- Green: fluorescent overhead lighting
- Magenta: mixed LED and flash

### Shadow quality

Professional lighting produces gentle shadows that shape the
subject without obscuring detail. Amateur lighting produces
either no shadow (flat and lifeless) or harsh shadow (flash
directly on subject).

## Background-Subject Isolation

Background assessment follows the "what else is in the frame"
test. The fewer competing elements, the stronger the
isolation.

### Clutter penalty tiers

- Zero clutter (seamless backdrop): baseline 10/10
- Clean surface + clean wall: 8/10
- Home environment visible but organized: 6/10
- Visible clutter (other items, laundry, toys): 4/10
- Chaotic environment: 2/10

### Seamless backdrop bonus

A seamless backdrop (white paper roll, gray muslin, neutral
wall with no horizon line) signals seller investment. This
alone lifts a listing above the 70th percentile for visual
quality on consumer marketplaces.

### The "garage floor" anti-pattern

A common amateur pattern: photograph item on garage floor or
driveway. Readable but signals "didn't care." Recommend
portable backdrop (even a bedsheet on wall) as a 5-minute fix.

## Professional vs Amateur Hallmarks

Specialist assessment distinguishes pro from amateur work by
visible markers.

### Professional hallmarks

- Consistent white balance across the photo set (no cast
  variation between shots)
- Shallow depth-of-field used intentionally on detail shots
- Rule-of-thirds compositional placement
- Catchlight in reflective surfaces (watches, polished metal,
  glass) indicating controlled lighting
- Color-checker or gray card visible in one photo (extreme
  pro)
- Subject fills 70-80 percent of frame consistently

### Amateur hallmarks

- Mixed white balance across photos (different lighting per
  shot)
- Everything in deep focus (point-and-shoot or phone default)
- Centered composition with excessive headroom
- Flash reflection on glossy surfaces
- Auto-rotate orientation errors
- Timestamp overlay in corner (rare but disqualifying)

## Watermark and Stock-Image Detection

A specialist-grade red flag. The 4-AI panel scans for:

- Visible watermarks (Shutterstock, Getty, Alamy, Depositphotos)
- Stock-photo aesthetic signatures (studio lighting too perfect
  for a resale listing, no visible dust, no scuffs even on
  claimed used item)
- Reverse-image-search hits from public catalog imagery
- Manufacturer product shots (unedited press photos with
  transparent background conversion)

When detected, flag as PROVENANCE CONCERN in conditionDocumentation
and recommend seller provide their own in-hand photos before
listing. This is a scam-prevention layer — watermarked or stock
imagery on a resale listing is a classic bait-and-switch signal.

## Seven Domain Archetypes

Every item maps to one of seven pricing archetypes. The archetype
determines which photo-coverage rules apply most aggressively and
which missing-shots categories are non-negotiable.

### 1. Musical Instruments (THE TEST FIXTURE)

The Dean MLX electric guitar is the canonical test case. Musical-
instrument photography discipline:

**Required photo set (Dean MLX example):**
1. Full body, front, against seamless backdrop
2. Full body, back
3. Headstock, front, angled to show logo and tuner layout
4. Headstock, back, showing serial number (close enough to
   read the characters)
5. Neck joint (heel) close-up — evidence of originality or
   modification
6. Pickup close-up — PAF-style vs active pickup is a material
   price signal
7. Any damage close-up — fret wear, finish checking, buckle
   rash, dings
8. (Bonus) Case and case candy if present — significant value
   add

**MI-specific photo tips:**
- Always shoot neck joint — evidence of originality is a
  major buyer concern for vintage or reissue instruments
- Always capture truss-rod cover — some have matching serial
  numbers or signatures
- Show pickup close-ups — pickup swaps affect value 15-25%
- Document finish checking or crazing if present — pre-1980
  pieces often have it and collectors value it as proof of age
- On acoustic instruments: photograph inside the sound hole
  to show label and brace construction

### 2. Antique Furniture

**Required photo set:**
1. Front elevation
2. Back elevation
3. Left side
4. Right side
5. Top (if horizontal surface)
6. Underside or interior base
7. Joinery close-up (dovetails, mortise-and-tenon, pegs)
8. Hardware close-up (handles, hinges, escutcheons)
9. Drawer open (if drawers present)
10. Any damage or repair close-up

### 3. Jewelry

**Required photo set:**
1. Top-down on neutral background
2. Side view
3. Back or clasp view
4. Hallmark macro (stamp readable)
5. Scale reference (coin, ruler, hand)
6. On-body or in-use context (optional but recommended)

### 4. Ceramics / Glass / Decorative Arts

**Required photo set:**
1. Front
2. Back
3. Base with maker mark
4. Interior view (where applicable)
5. Any chip, crack, or hairline close-up
6. Scale reference

### 5. Vehicles (handled by CarBot but PhotoBot cross-references)

**Required photo set:**
10+ photos including all 4 corners, engine bay, mileage
close-up, VIN plate, tire tread, interior dashboard, seats,
cargo area, underside (where possible), any damage.

### 6. Clothing / Textiles

**Required photo set:**
1. Front on hanger or flat-lay
2. Back
3. Label and size tag
4. Close-up of fabric texture
5. Any wear, stain, or defect close-up
6. Closure or hardware (zipper, buttons, clasp)

### 7. Electronics

**Required photo set:**
1. Front with screen
2. Back
3. Ports and connections
4. Screen powered on (critical proof of function)
5. Serial number plate
6. Any damage close-up
