# Composition Rules

## Composition as a Value Signal

The way a photo is framed communicates the seller's competence and the item's importance. This is not a soft claim. Buyers make price anchoring judgments within the first two seconds of viewing a product photo, and those judgments are heavily influenced by how the photo is composed.

A well-composed photo signals that the seller understands the item has value and treated the photography accordingly. A carelessly composed photo signals the opposite. The same object, photographed at the same time in the same lighting, can appear to be worth $50 or $200 depending solely on composition decisions.

This effect is measurable in resale auction data. Items listed with professionally composed photos — clean background, correct fill rate, level horizon, correct orientation — sell at 15 to 35 percent higher final prices than identical items listed with snapshot-quality composition, controlling for condition and description quality. Composition is not aesthetic preference. It is a financial lever.

## The 60-80 Percent Fill Rule

The item should occupy between 60 and 80 percent of the frame area by visual weight in the hero photo.

Below 60 percent fill, the item appears small and visually unimportant. The buyer's eye must search for the item within the frame. The surrounding empty space competes with the item for visual attention and loses — but the competition itself degrades the photo. Items that appear small in photos appear cheap regardless of actual size or value.

Above 80 percent fill, critical features get cropped at the frame edges. The buyer cannot assess the item's full form, proportions, or condition. High fill rates also often indicate that the photographer was too close to the item, producing barrel distortion from a wide-angle phone lens that distorts proportions.

The 60-80 percent target applies to the hero shot and front-facing angle shots. Close-up detail shots, maker mark shots, and condition documentation shots intentionally fill the frame differently — a maker mark shot may fill nearly 100 percent of the frame with the mark area, and that is correct for its purpose.

## Center-Weighted Framing for Resale

Unlike landscape photography, fine art photography, or editorial photography, resale product photography benefits from center-weighted framing. The item should be centered, or nearly centered, in both horizontal and vertical axes.

The rule of thirds — placing the subject at one of the intersection points of a thirds grid — is excellent advice for artistic photography but counterproductive for product listings. Off-center product shots create ambiguity about whether the item is intentionally positioned or carelessly photographed. They also create dead space on one side of the frame that reduces effective fill rate and makes the item appear smaller than it is.

For items that have a strong directionality — a car facing left, a pitcher with a handle on one side, a chair angled in three-quarter view — a slight offset toward the item's visual center of mass is acceptable. The item's visual center of mass is not always its geometric center. A teapot's visual center of mass is at the body, not the handle, so the body should be centered even if this places the handle near the edge of the frame.

## Dead Space and Perceived Value

Dead space is empty area in the frame that contains neither the item nor informative background content. It occurs most frequently at the top of the frame when items are photographed from a slightly elevated angle, and at the sides of the frame when photographers back away from an item that does not fill a horizontal frame.

Dead space communicates "this item is small." The brain interprets large empty areas surrounding an object as indicating that the object required that much space around it to be safely photographed — that is, it is delicate, small, or insignificant.

Eliminating dead space by moving the camera closer or zooming in (using optical zoom, not digital zoom) increases the item's apparent importance without changing the item itself.

Dead space at the top of the frame is particularly damaging because it forces the item downward in the frame, creating an unanchored visual effect. Items should be centered vertically with approximately equal space above and below.

## Item Orientation Standards

Horizontal items — tables, benches, rugs, long swords, horizontal paintings, vehicles — must be photographed in landscape orientation. Photographing a horizontal item in portrait orientation wastes roughly 40 percent of the frame on empty space at the top and bottom.

Vertical items — lamps, vases, grandfather clocks, standing figures, tall paintings — must be photographed in portrait orientation. A vertical item photographed in landscape orientation is reduced to a small figure in the center of a wide frame.

Square items — plates, square paintings, boxes, some clocks — photograph effectively in either orientation but perform best in square crop, which most platforms support and which eliminates all dead space.

When selling on platforms that require square crops — eBay's default display is square, Instagram is square — non-square items require careful centering to ensure the square crop does not remove critical features.

## The Tilt Problem

A tilted product photo is one of the most consistently penalizing composition errors in resale photography. Even two to three degrees of tilt communicates instability, carelessness, or damage.

The specific perception triggered by tilt is context-dependent. A tilted photo of a vase suggests the vase may be cracked or unstable. A tilted photo of a painting suggests the frame may be warped. A tilted photo of a watch suggests it was photographed carelessly by someone who did not have time to align the shot. All of these associations reduce perceived value.

Tilt occurs most frequently when photographers shoot from a handheld phone without a stable grip, when items are placed on uneven surfaces that the camera aligns to rather than the true horizontal, and when the photographer is standing at an angle to the item rather than directly in front of it.

The correction is to use the gridlines on the phone camera — universally available in both iPhone and Android camera apps — and align horizontal features of the item to the horizontal gridlines before shooting. For items without clear horizontal reference lines, such as abstract sculptures or irregular rocks, the base of the item should be aligned to the bottom of the frame rather than attempting to level an item that has no true horizontal.

Tilt above five degrees in either direction is a flagging condition. The photo should be rejected or corrected.

## Mobile Phone Composition Tips

Most resale photos are taken on mobile phones. The following guidance is specific to mobile photography.

Enable gridlines. On iPhone, this is in Settings under Camera. On Android, it is in the camera app settings. Gridlines enable precise centering and horizon alignment without estimation.

Shoot at eye level to the item's center. This is the most natural and flattering angle for most three-dimensional objects. Shooting from above makes items look small. Shooting from below makes items look distorted and exaggerated. Eye level to the item's midpoint is the neutral, flattering angle.

Use optical zoom, not digital zoom. On multi-lens iPhones and modern Android devices, switching to the 2x or 3x camera is optical zoom — physically moving to a longer focal length. Digital zoom is crop-and-enlarge, which reduces image quality. For small items, the 2x or 3x lens also reduces the barrel distortion produced by wide-angle lenses that makes items appear bloated and unnatural.

Lock focus and exposure by pressing and holding on the item before shooting. This prevents the camera's autofocus from shifting to the background after you have positioned the shot.

Use a stable surface or prop when possible. Leaning a phone against a book stack and using the self-timer produces sharper images than handheld shooting, particularly in lower light where shutter speeds are slower.

## Aspect Ratio by Platform

Choosing the correct aspect ratio for the target platform ensures the hero photo displays without unwanted cropping in platform interfaces.

eBay displays hero photos at 1:1 square in search results. Composing at 4:3 (standard phone photo) means the platform will center-crop to square. If the item is not centered in the original 4:3 photo, the square crop may cut important features. Compose center-weighted in 4:3, or shoot in 1:1 square mode, to prevent cropping surprises.

Etsy displays hero photos at 4:5 portrait in browse results and 1:1 in some grid views. A 4:5 composition gives maximum control over how the photo displays. Landscape items photographed in landscape orientation will have significant side areas cropped in Etsy's portrait display.

Facebook Marketplace displays at 4:5 in the feed and 1:1 in thumbnails. The same center-weighted composition recommendation applies.

1stDibs uses portrait and landscape as submitted. Shoot the correct orientation for the item and do not rely on the platform to reformat.

Poshmark uses 1:1 square displays exclusively. All clothing items should be composed with square display in mind.

## Composition Score

Rate composition on a scale of 1 to 10.

A score of 9 to 10 means correct fill rate, level horizon, correct orientation, no dead space, appropriate centering, correct aspect ratio for the target platform. No adjustments needed.

A score of 7 to 8 means one minor issue — slight off-center, small amount of dead space, two to three degrees of tilt — that does not materially harm the listing.

A score of 5 to 6 means a correctable problem — noticeable dead space, item too small in frame, noticeable tilt — that reduces click rate and should be corrected before the listing is promoted.

A score of 4 or below means a fundamental composition problem — item fills less than 40 percent of the frame, severe tilt, wrong orientation, critical features cropped — that requires a reshoot.
