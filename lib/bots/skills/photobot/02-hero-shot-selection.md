# Hero Shot Selection

## Why the Hero Photo Is the Only Photo That Matters First

Buyers browsing a marketplace grid page make a click-or-scroll decision in under two seconds. In that window, they are not reading your title, your price, or your condition notes. They are looking at one image — the hero shot. Every other photo in the listing exists to close the sale after the hero shot has already opened the door.

A poor hero photo on a $500 item produces fewer clicks than a great hero photo on a $50 item. The hero shot is the single highest-leverage variable in resale photography. It determines traffic. Traffic determines offers. Offers determine final sale price.

## What Makes a Hero Shot

A hero photo must satisfy all of the following criteria simultaneously.

The item must be centered in the frame. Slight off-center framing is acceptable for artistic effect on lifestyle platforms, but for marketplace resale the center-weighted composition communicates clarity and confidence. Buyers do not want to hunt for the item within the frame.

The item must fill between 60 and 80 percent of the frame. Below 60 percent and the item looks small, unimportant, or lost. Above 80 percent and important edges or contextual features get cropped out, raising questions about what the seller is hiding.

Focus must be sharp at the primary surface of the item. The lens should be focused on the face or front plane of the item, not the background, not an edge, not a reflection. Soft focus on a hero photo is an instant disqualifier.

Colors must be accurate to the item as it appears in person under daylight. The hero photo sets buyer expectations. If the colors are shifted by poor white balance, the buyer receives the item and perceives it as misrepresented, even if every other detail was accurate. Color accuracy in the hero shot is a trust signal.

Lighting must be even and diffused. Harsh shadows splitting the item in half, specular highlights that blow out detail on reflective surfaces, or backlighting that silhouettes the item against a bright window are all disqualifying conditions for a hero shot.

The background must be clean and non-competing. Clutter behind the item pulls the eye away from the item. A neutral background — white, off-white, gray, or a contextually appropriate surface — keeps visual attention on the product.

## Platform-Specific Hero Preferences

Different platforms have different expectations for the hero photo, and violating those expectations reduces trust even if the photo itself is technically well-executed.

eBay favors white or near-white backgrounds for the hero shot. The platform's category search results are displayed against a white interface, and items shot against white integrate visually into the platform. eBay's image policies also formally recommend white backgrounds. An item shot against wood grain or fabric on eBay looks out of place and slightly unprofessional.

Etsy favors styled or lifestyle hero shots. Buyers on Etsy are purchasing an aesthetic experience as much as an object. A vintage ceramic mug photographed on a linen cloth beside a small plant performs better than the same mug on a white sweep. The hero photo on Etsy should help the buyer envision the item in their home.

Facebook Marketplace favors bright, high-contrast images that read clearly at small thumbnail sizes. The Facebook mobile feed compresses images aggressively. Muted, low-contrast, or complex hero photos lose all definition at thumbnail scale. High ambient brightness and clear subject separation from the background are essential.

1stDibs and other premium platforms require gallery-quality hero photography. Images must be lit to the standard of auction house catalog photography — even, shadowless, accurate color, sharp across the full item, and shot with sufficient resolution to support large-format zoom. A snapshot-quality photo on a premium platform signals to buyers that the seller does not understand the value of what they are selling.

## The Hero-or-Not Decision Tree

When evaluating a set of photos to select the hero, apply the following decision logic in sequence.

First, eliminate any photo that is blurry, out of focus, or motion-blurred. A blurry photo cannot be the hero regardless of any other attribute.

Second, eliminate any photo where the item occupies less than 50 percent of the frame. The item is not readable at thumbnail size and the photo will not generate clicks.

Third, eliminate any photo with a cluttered background containing recognizable objects, other items, hands, or identifiable personal spaces.

Fourth, eliminate any photo with severe color cast — orange, yellow, blue, or green shifts that misrepresent the item's actual color.

Fifth, eliminate any photo that is tilted more than three degrees from level. Tilt suggests instability, damage, or careless handling. Buyers unconsciously associate tilt with items that are broken or less valuable.

Sixth, from the remaining candidates, rank by fill rate (prefer 70 percent over 55 percent), then by lighting quality (prefer diffused even light over directional shadows), then by background cleanliness.

The highest-ranked remaining photo is the recommended hero shot. If no photos survive the elimination steps, flag the entire set as requiring a reshoot before listing.

## What Disqualifies a Photo from Hero Status

These conditions are absolute disqualifiers. No amount of cropping, brightness adjustment, or filtering can rescue a photo with these problems.

A tilted horizon line. A blurry subject. A hand or finger in the frame. Another item from the seller's inventory visible in the background. Harsh shadow splitting the item. Lens flare. The item photographed from so far away that it is a small object floating in empty space. The item cut off at the frame edge. The item photographed from an angle so steep that the primary face is not visible.

Marginal problems — slight shadows, slightly off-center framing, slight background imperfection — are acceptable in a hero shot only when no better candidate exists in the set.

## Scoring the Hero Shot

Rate the selected hero shot on a scale of 1 to 10.

A score of 9 to 10 means the photo would be accepted without modification by any major resale platform and is ready for listing.

A score of 7 to 8 means the photo is good enough to list but has one minor issue — slight shadow, slightly off-center — that a reshoot would improve.

A score of 5 to 6 means the photo is acceptable as a temporary placeholder but should be replaced before the item is promoted or featured.

A score of 4 or below means the photo actively hurts the listing and should be replaced before publishing.

## Communicating Hero Shot Recommendations

When reporting to the seller, identify the recommended hero shot by photo number or filename. Explain specifically why it was selected over other candidates. If the recommended hero shot scores below 7, explain specifically what would need to change in a reshoot to achieve a score of 9 or higher.

Do not tell sellers their photos are bad without telling them exactly how to make them better.
