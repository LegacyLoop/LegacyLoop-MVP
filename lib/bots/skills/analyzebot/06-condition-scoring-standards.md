# Condition Scoring Standards

## The Foundation: Photo-Only Assessment

Every condition score produced by AnalyzeBot is a photograph-based estimate, not an in-hand inspection. This distinction is not merely a disclaimer — it is a structural limitation that must be built into the score itself.

When assessing condition from photographs only, apply a one-point uncertainty deduction from what you would assign if you were holding the piece. You can see the top surface but not the underside. You can see one side of a drawer but not the interior. You can see the finish in bright light but not how it feels, whether it is stable, or whether it has a surface treatment that looks good in photographs but is flaking. This inherent limitation is encoded as the standard photo-assessment deduction.

State the photo-only basis in every condition assessment. "Condition score 7/10 based on available photographs. In-hand inspection may reveal issues not visible from provided images."

## The 10-Point Scale Defined

### 10 — Mint or New Old Stock

Unused or appears unused. No wear, no scratches, no patina beyond what is inherent to the material. Original packaging if applicable. For antiques, a score of 10 is nearly impossible because genuine age always produces some evidence of time. For items described as "never used" from an estate, a 10 may be appropriate if no evidence of use is visible in any photograph.

### 9 — Excellent

Near-perfect condition. Only the most careful examination reveals any evidence of use. For a silver piece, this means complete original finish with only the faintest surface hairlines from normal polishing. For furniture, all original finish intact with no chips, no water rings, no significant scratches. For ceramics, no chips, cracks, hairlines, or crazing in the glaze. For textiles, no fading, no staining, no fraying.

### 8 — Very Good

Minor wear consistent with careful use and age. A silver piece shows light surface scratches from normal polishing but no dents or deep scratches. Furniture shows minor scratches, perhaps one small ring mark, but no structural damage and substantially original finish. Ceramics show no chips but may have very minor crazing or minute surface wear at rims. Textiles show minimal fading and no significant staining.

### 7 — Good

Noticeable wear but non-structural. The piece is complete and presentable. A silver piece shows noticeable surface scratches and light buffing marks but no dents or significant damage. Furniture shows multiple scratches, light water rings, minor finish wear at edges and corners, but the surface is stable and the structure is sound. Ceramics may show minor chips to rims, small hairline cracks, or minor crazing but nothing that affects structural integrity. Textiles show some fading or minor staining that does not detract significantly.

### 6 — Fair

Clear and obvious signs of use that affect the visual presentation, though the piece remains complete and functional. Furniture shows significant finish wear, multiple water rings, or a clearly worn or partially refinished surface. Silver shows dents, significant scratches, or evidence of old repairs. Ceramics may show repaired cracks, chips to significant areas, or significant crazing. Textiles show obvious fading, staining, or minor tears.

### 5 — Below Average

Significant wear, damage, or alteration that substantially reduces appeal and value. Furniture with a stripped and poorly refinished surface, replaced hardware, missing veneer, or structural wobble. Silver with significant denting, repairs, or missing elements (missing lid, missing insert). Ceramics with major chips, large hairline cracks, or significant repairs that are visible. Textiles with moth damage, significant staining, tears, or heavy fading.

### 4 — Poor

Damaged but functional or at least structurally intact. A chair that is wobbly and has significant finish damage but could be repaired. A silver piece with multiple repairs and significant loss of original surface. A ceramic piece with a significant crack that has been repaired (and the repair is visible). Textiles with holes, major staining, or significant structural damage to the weave.

### 3 — Needs Restoration

Requires professional restoration before it would be appropriate for display or use. Structural damage, significant missing elements, or extensive surface loss. The piece is identifiable and complete enough to warrant restoration but is not presentable in its current state.

### 2 — Parts or Salvage Value

Missing major elements, extensively damaged, or otherwise useful primarily as a parts source or for salvage of specific components. A chair with a broken frame and missing seat. A silver set missing multiple pieces. A ceramic piece in multiple fragments.

### 1 — Severely Damaged

Fundamental structural failure or loss so extensive that the piece has minimal value beyond material or curiosity interest. Reserved for the most extreme cases of damage or deterioration.

## Cosmetic vs. Functional Scores

Every condition assessment produces two sub-scores: cosmetic and functional. These can diverge significantly and that divergence is itself information.

Cosmetic score measures visual appearance. Scratches, stains, fading, finish condition, color consistency, and visible damage all affect the cosmetic score. A piece can have perfect cosmetic presentation while having hidden functional problems.

Functional score measures usability and mechanical integrity. For furniture: does it sit flat, do the drawers operate smoothly, does the structure bear weight without wobble, do the hinges work? For silver hollow ware: is the form intact, does the lid fit, does it pour without leaking? For clocks: does the movement operate? For textiles: is the fabric structurally sound?

A cosmetic score of 8 and a functional score of 5 describes a piece that looks good in photographs but has structural or mechanical problems. This is an important signal for both pricing and buyer expectation management.

A cosmetic score of 5 and a functional score of 8 describes a piece that looks rough but works well. This may be a strong candidate for restoration that would substantially increase value.

When cosmetic and functional scores differ by 3 or more points, flag this divergence explicitly and note the implication.

## Assessing Condition from Photographs: Key Observation Points

### Edges and Corners

Edge wear is the most reliable indicator of honest use. On furniture, the front corners of tabletops, the top corners of drawer fronts, and the feet should show wear proportional to claimed age. Sharp, crisp edges on a piece claimed to be 150 years old are a red flag. Rounded, worn edges on a piece claimed to be new suggest misrepresentation. Edges show wear before flat surfaces do.

### Surfaces and Finish

Look for the finish condition across the entire photographed surface. Shellac finishes (pre-1920s) show a specific crazing pattern when old. Lacquer finishes (post-1920s) crack and peel in distinct patterns. Wax and oil finishes show wear at use points and build-up in recesses. Look for clouding (water damage to old shellac), blushing (moisture trapped in finish), dark stains (water or chemical), and scratches. A surface that appears too even and bright may indicate recent refinishing.

### Moving Parts

Assess drawer alignment from photographs. A drawer that shows a slight gap at one corner, or whose front is not perfectly aligned with the surrounding frame, suggests the case has racked or the drawer has swollen. This is natural in antiques but must be noted. Hinge gaps in cabinet doors, lid misalignment on boxes, and lid fit on silver hollow ware are all visible in photographs if the item is photographed with lids open and closed.

### Completeness

Look for evidence of missing elements. Hardware with ghost marks (darker areas where a pull used to be, unfaded wood where a piece was not exposed to light) indicates hardware removal. Hinge impressions without hinges indicate hinge removal. A ceramic piece with a replaced lid (different glaze, different weight appearance, slightly different proportion) is not complete in the auction sense.

## Condition Score to Value Impact

Each condition point on the 10-point scale represents approximately a 10-15% value differential for typical items. Moving from a 7 to an 8 increases value by approximately 10-15%. Moving from a 4 to a 7 (restoration result) may increase value by 30-45%.

For high-value items (estimated value over $500), condition differences of a single point can represent meaningful dollar amounts. A score of 8 versus a score of 7 on a $2,000 piece is a $200-300 difference.

For items estimated at very high value (over $5,000), condition differences of half a point can represent significant amounts. At this level, condition scoring must be treated as preliminary and the note "professional in-hand appraisal strongly recommended for items of this estimated value" should be added.

## Specialist Routing Based on Condition

A condition score of 5 or below on a high-value item (estimated value over $300) triggers conservative pricing. The lower bound of the estimated price range should reflect the current condition, and a restoration value estimate should be offered separately. "Current condition value: $150-200. Estimated restored value if professionally restored: $400-500. Restoration cost estimate: $100-200."

A condition score of 9 or 10 on an item that should logically show age-related wear triggers a reproduction check flag. Newly made reproductions often appear in perfect condition. The note should read: "Unusually high condition for estimated age. Verify age dating against construction evidence. Consider whether item may be a reproduction of the attributed period."

A condition score where cosmetic and functional diverge by 3 or more points should trigger a specific notation: "Cosmetic condition [X], functional condition [Y]. Divergence of [Z] points suggests [specific condition description]. Buyer should be informed of [functional issue] before purchase."
