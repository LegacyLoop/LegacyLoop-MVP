# Era and Style Attribution

## The Purpose of Era Attribution

Era attribution establishes the manufacturing period and thereby determines whether an item qualifies as antique (age 100 years or more by traditional auction house standard, or 50 years for LegacyLoop's antique flagging threshold), a vintage item (20-100 years), or a contemporary piece. It also anchors market comparables to the correct collector category and informs pricing expectations.

Era attribution combines style movement identification with construction technique dating and hardware analysis. All three must be assessed. When they agree, confidence is high. When they conflict, a more complex story is indicated: a revival piece, a reproduction, a later restoration with period-inappropriate materials, or an outright fake.

## Style Movement Identification and Date Ranges

### William and Mary, 1690-1725

Trumpet-turned legs, high relief carving, oyster veneering, inverted cup turnings, Spanish feet. High chests and daybeds are characteristic forms. Primary wood is walnut in England and America.

### Queen Anne, 1710-1750

Cabriole leg with pad foot (American), spoon back chair with vase-shaped splat, bonnet tops on case pieces, shell carving at the knee and crest. Restrained elegance compared to William and Mary. Walnut dominant in England; walnut and maple in New England.

### Chippendale, 1755-1790

Ball-and-claw foot (the signature element), pierced ladder-back or gothic splats on chairs, broken pediment tops on case pieces, acanthus leaf carving on knees, cabriole legs with more elaborate carving than Queen Anne. Philadelphia Chippendale is among the most valuable American furniture. Mahogany dominant.

### Federal and Hepplewhite, 1790-1810

Tapered, square-section legs (a defining Federal indicator replacing the cabriole). Inlay in contrasting woods (stringing, banding, paterae, bellflower chains). Lighter, more restrained carving than Chippendale. Shield-back chairs. Serpentine and bowfront case pieces. Mahogany primary, satinwood and fruitwood for inlay.

### Sheraton, 1800-1820

Similar to Hepplewhite but with turned, reeded legs. Brass ring pulls replacing the bail pull. Rectilinear forms predominate. Card tables with the characteristic "astragal end" shape. The period name overlaps with Federal.

### American Empire, 1820-1840

Heavy, imposing forms. Columns (turned or carved), paw feet, massive proportions. Crotch mahogany veneers on broad flat surfaces. Gilded stencil decoration on painted surfaces (fancy chairs). The period coincides with the transition from hand-forged to cut nails.

### Victorian, 1840-1900

Broadly defined by the reign of Queen Victoria. Sub-periods include Early Victorian (heavy and dark), Renaissance Revival (architectural pediments and incised linear ornament), Eastlake (geometric, incised ornament, reform aesthetic), and Rococo Revival (scrolling, naturalistic carving). Primary woods darken: walnut, rosewood, and ebonized finishes. Machinery increasingly produces carved ornament that appears uniform.

### Arts and Crafts, 1895-1920

Quartersawn white oak showing prominent ray fleck is the signature material. Exposed joinery (mortise-and-tenon with visible wooden pegs, exposed tenon ends through the surface of legs and rails). Hammered copper hardware. Simple, rectilinear forms. Mission furniture is an American variant; Stickley is the most collected American Arts and Crafts maker. Greene and Greene in California produced the highest-art American interpretation. English makers include Voysey and Gimson.

### Art Nouveau, 1890-1910

Organic, flowing, natural forms. Whiplash curves. Female figures with flowing hair. Lily pads, irises, dragonflies, and peacock feathers as motifs. Tiffany glass is the American icon. French furniture makers in the Nancy and Paris schools. The style was international and simultaneous across multiple media.

### Art Deco, 1920-1940

Geometric forms, sunburst motifs, stepped profiles, chevrons, zigzags. Chrome and glass as primary materials for American Deco. Lacquer and exotic veneers (zebrawood, macassar ebony, amboyna) for French high Deco. Bakelite (first commercial plastic, patented 1909, widespread by late 1920s) in combination with chrome is a date anchor. Streamline Moderne (after about 1934) softens the geometry into aerodynamic curves.

### Mid-Century Modern, 1945-1975

Tapered, angled legs (a defining visual indicator). Teak, walnut, and rosewood as primary woods in furniture. Organic sculptural forms in the Scandinavian tradition (Eames, Wegner, Jacobsen, Bertoia, Saarinen). Low-profile forms suited to modern interiors. Molded plywood and plastic in experimental pieces. This period is heavily collected and has many reproductions; construction quality and original hardware are critical authentication points.

### Post-Modern and Contemporary, 1975-present

Items falling in this period are not antique by LegacyLoop's 50-year threshold. They may qualify for collectible status if by significant designers or manufacturers.

## Construction Technique Dating

### Dovetail Dating

Hand-cut dovetails show characteristic irregularity: pins and tails of varying width, chisel marks at the baseline, slight off-plumb cutting. The scribe line (the baseline marked with a marking gauge before cutting) is visible as a fine incised line around the drawer interior. Hand-cut dovetails are consistent with pre-1860 manufacture in American furniture.

Machine-cut dovetails (post-1860) are perfectly regular in spacing and angle. Machine regularity is absolute; hand-cutting always introduces variation. The presence of any variation indicates hand work; perfect regularity indicates machine work.

Router-cut dovetails, used in modern furniture, produce a characteristic rounded valley between each tail. This is definitively modern.

### Panel Construction and Shrinkage

Frame-and-panel construction (a solid wood panel floating in a grooved frame) is found from medieval work through the twentieth century. What dates it is the evidence of movement. Old solid wood panels shrink across the grain over decades and centuries. A panel with visible gaps at one side, or a crack across the panel face, or a panel that has pulled slightly from the groove on one side, shows real wood movement over real time. Modern furniture built with plywood or MDF panels does not exhibit this movement.

## Hardware and Fastener Dating

### Nail Dating

Hand-forged nails have square shanks that taper to a point on all four sides. The head is irregular and formed by hammer blows; no two are identical. Hand-forged nails indicate pre-1790 manufacture. They are found in furniture back panels, in the attachment of wooden elements, and in structural framing.

Cut nails have a rectangular shank that tapers in only one plane (like a blunted rectangle). The head is more regular than hand-forged but still shows some variation. Cut nails were produced by machinery from about 1790 and dominated through most of the nineteenth century. Cut nails in furniture indicate 1790-1890 manufacture.

Round wire nails have a perfectly circular shank of consistent diameter. They were patented in 1867 and became commercially dominant by the 1890s. Round wire nails in a piece that claims pre-Civil War manufacture are a red flag.

### Screw Dating

Pre-1850 hand-cut screws have off-center slots (the slot is not perfectly centered in the head) and blunt tips (the thread does not come to a sharp point at the end). Machine-cut screws from 1850 onward have centered slots and sharper tips. Phillips-head screws were invented in 1936 and became commercially widespread by 1940. A Phillips-head screw in any construction claiming pre-1936 manufacture is definitive evidence of replacement or later manufacture.

### Pull Hardware Dating

Hand-cast brass bail pulls are consistent with eighteenth-century manufacture. The casting shows slight surface irregularities; the bails are slightly irregular in form. Stamped brass pulls (later) are more uniform. The style of pull (butterfly, bat's-wing, bail, ring, wooden knob, glass knob, pressed glass knob) carries its own period associations. Glass knobs became fashionable in the Empire period and remained common through the Victorian era.

## Era Confidence Scoring

Confidence in era attribution begins at 50 points and adjusts based on corroborating or conflicting evidence.

When three or more independent dating features agree (for example, the style is consistent with Mission Arts and Crafts, the construction shows hand-cut mortise-and-tenon joinery with visible peg ends, and the hardware is hammered copper), the confidence is high: 80 or above. The features independently point to the same period, making coincidental reproduction less likely.

When features conflict (for example, the visual style matches 1780 Chippendale but the construction shows round wire nails and machine-cut dovetails), the confidence in any single attribution is low, and the conflict must be reported explicitly. Possible explanations: the piece is a revival (Chippendale Revival was popular in the 1870s-1880s), the piece is a reproduction, the piece was significantly restored using later materials, or the piece is a marriage of an old top and a new base.

## How Era Determination Triggers Antique Detection

The antique flag (is_antique=true) is triggered when the estimated manufacturing date is 50 or more years before the current analysis date. The current analysis year is 2026. Therefore, any item estimated to have been manufactured in 1976 or earlier triggers the antique flag.

If visual dating places a piece firmly in the Mid-Century Modern period (1945-1975), the piece may or may not meet the 50-year threshold. Use the estimated year range: if the lower end of the range is 50+ years ago, flag as antique.

If visual dating is uncertain but places a piece in any named style period entirely predating 1976, flag as antique and populate the antique_markers array with the specific visual evidence that supports the period attribution.

When three or more independent dating features from different categories (style, construction, hardware) agree on a period that predates 1976, the is_antique flag must be set regardless of the absence of explicit documentation. The visual evidence is itself the evidence.
