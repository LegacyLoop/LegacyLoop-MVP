# Visual Identification Mastery

## The Core Principle

The camera captures pixels. The expert reads construction era, regional origin, maker quality, and condition history from those pixels. Every photograph contains more information than its surface appearance. Your task is to extract that latent information systematically before forming any identification conclusion.

## Reading Materials from Visual Cues

### Wood

Oak presents prominent medullary rays, visible as flecks or ribbons in quartersawn surfaces. Walnut shows a swirling, irregular grain with a chocolate-to-gray color range. Mahogany presents straight, tight grain with a reddish-brown base tone. Pine shows wide, soft grain with resin pockets. Cherry starts pale and deepens with age and light exposure to a rich amber. Rosewood is dense, dark, and often streaked with black. Satinwood is pale yellow with a fine, lustrous grain. Ebony is nearly black with minimal visible grain.

Old-growth wood, cut before 1900 in most North American contexts, shows tight annual rings because the trees grew slowly in competition. Plantation-growth wood shows wider, faster rings. Ring tightness visible at end-grain exposure or in a cross-section is a significant age indicator.

### Metal

Brass appears warm yellow and develops a green or blue-green patina (verdigris) in recesses. Cast brass is heavier than stamped brass; look for seam lines indicating stamped construction. Copper is distinctly reddish and develops green verdigris. Silver is bright white with a gray-black tarnish pattern. Pewter is a soft, muted gray, lighter than silver in visual tone. Chrome is mirror-bright and was not commercially viable before the 1920s; any chrome-plated item is post-1920. Nickel is silver-gray and harder in appearance than pewter. Japanned metal has a black lacquer finish common on Victorian ironware. Ormolu is gilt bronze, warm golden.

### Ceramic and Glass

Porcelain transmits light when thin and rings clearly when struck. Bone china is the whitest and lightest. Stoneware is dense, opaque, and makes a thudding sound. Earthenware is porous; unglazed bases show the clay body color. Look for the pontil mark on antique glass (a rough circular scar on the base where the glassblower's rod was detached). Mold seams in glass indicate post-1820 manufacture; seams that stop before the lip indicate hand-finishing (pre-1904). Seams that run through the lip indicate fully automated production (post-1904).

### Textile

Silk has a natural sheen and drapes with fluidity. Wool appears matte and has visible weight. Cotton lies flatter and lacks the sheen of silk. Linen shows a characteristic slubbed texture from the flax fibers. Velvet pile direction is visible as a tonal shift when viewed from different angles in the photograph. Machine-woven textiles show perfectly even weave; hand-woven textiles show slight irregularities.

## Construction Analysis from Photographs

Joinery is among the most reliable dating indicators. Hand-cut dovetails show slight irregularities: pins and tails of uneven width, saw marks visible on the faces, chisel marks at the baseline. Machine-cut dovetails are perfectly regular. Router-cut dovetails are perfectly regular and evenly spaced; this is modern construction.

Examine nail heads at joints. Square-headed hand-forged nails indicate pre-1790 manufacture. Cut nails with rectangular shanks indicate 1790-1890. Round wire nails indicate post-1890. Screws with off-center slots and blunt tips indicate pre-1850 hand-cut screws. Machine-cut screws with centered slots began around 1850. Phillips-head screws were not commercially available until 1936.

Secondary surfaces (drawer bottoms, back panels, underside of shelves) often preserve the most honest construction evidence. Circular saw marks indicate post-1830 manufacture. Up-and-down straight saw marks indicate earlier. Hand-plane marks visible as subtle scallops are consistent with pre-industrial construction.

## Wear Pattern Analysis

Honest wear concentrates at points of contact and use. On chair arms: the front outer corners and the areas where hands grip. On drawer pulls: the metal directly below the bail or knob. On tabletop surfaces: the areas directly in front of seat positions. On book spines: the top third where hands grasp when pulling from a shelf. On silver flatware: the bowl of spoons and the tines of forks.

Artificial aging distributes wear incorrectly. It appears on raised surfaces (the high points of carving) as if the piece was rubbed with an abrasive, rather than worn through actual use. Check edges: on a genuinely old piece, the sharpest edges are softened by handling. On a reproduction with artificial distressing, edges may be sharp while surfaces show fake wear marks.

Painted surfaces: genuine crackle (crazing) forms a network pattern across the entire surface. Painted-on crackle is applied in lines. Genuine paint wear reveals earlier layers or bare wood beneath at logical stress points. Repainted pieces sometimes show the original paint color in protected recesses.

## Patina Reading

Genuine patina is uneven and accumulates in recesses, corners, carvings, and areas that cannot be easily polished. It deepens in shadow areas and lightens on exposed high points. The color is consistent with the base material's natural oxidation chemistry.

Fake patina is often uniform in application. It may appear in places that would not logically accumulate oxidation. It may be the wrong color for the material (silver developing brown rather than gray-black tarnish). Wax-based fake patina catches light differently than natural oxidation.

For wood: genuine old finish (shellac, oil, wax) has a depth and warmth that sits within the surface. New finish sits on top of the surface and appears brighter. A piece stripped and refinished loses significant value; check for stain penetration into the wood grain in scratches or chips.

## Confidence Scoring for Identifications

Base confidence starts at 50 points for any identification made from a photograph.

Add points for each positive diagnostic feature observed: clear maker mark (+20), period-appropriate construction technique confirmed (+15), material identification confirmed (+10 per material), wear pattern consistent with stated age (+10), patina consistent with stated age (+10), provenance label or auction sticker visible (+15).

Subtract points for: low photo resolution preventing detail examination (-15), glare obscuring key surfaces (-10), no maker mark visible when one is expected (-5), conflicting dating indicators (style says one era, construction says another) (-20), evidence of refinishing or restoration (-10).

A confidence score of 70 or above supports a high-confidence identification. Between 50 and 70, report as moderate confidence. Below 50, report as low confidence and invoke the "more photos needed" protocol.

## The More Photos Needed Protocol

When you cannot confidently identify from available photos, do not guess. Name the specific additional photographs that would resolve the uncertainty.

For furniture: "A photograph of the drawer interior showing secondary wood and construction joinery would confirm the dating. A photograph of the back panel showing the panel attachment method would clarify whether this is pre- or post-industrial manufacture."

For silver and metal: "A photograph of the underside showing any maker stamps or hallmarks is required before identification can be confirmed."

For ceramics: "A photograph of the unglazed base showing the clay body color and any impressed or painted marks would allow maker attribution."

For textiles: "A close-up photograph of the weave structure at a corner or edge would allow fiber identification."

For paintings: "A photograph of the canvas or board back, the signature area with raking light if possible, and any gallery or auction labels would substantially improve identification confidence."

Always phrase the request in terms of what the photograph will tell you, not simply what you want to see. This helps the seller understand what to photograph and why it matters.

## What the Expert Reads That the Camera Cannot Directly Show

Weight is inferred from construction density visible in the photographs. Proportion is read against known standards for period and style. Rarity is assessed from knowledge of what survives from a period. Desirability is assessed from collector market knowledge. Regional attribution is made from style tradition, secondary wood species typical to a region, and construction conventions specific to regional furniture schools.

The expert interprets photographs through a framework built from thousands of reference points. Every identification is a hypothesis tested against the visual evidence. Report your reasoning, not just your conclusion.
