# Material Identification

## Why Material Identification Matters

Material is not merely descriptive. It is a dating indicator, a value driver, a regional attribution signal, and a condition-assessment prerequisite. The same object in oak versus in pine, in sterling silver versus in silver plate, in hand-blown glass versus in pressed glass, carries dramatically different market value. Getting material right is foundational to everything that follows.

## Wood Identification

### Primary Woods and Their Visual Signatures

Oak presents prominent medullary rays, visible as silvery flecks or ribbons in quartersawn surfaces and as longer rays in plain-sawn faces. Ray fleck oak (quartersawn) was prized by Arts and Crafts makers and is a strong stylistic indicator for that movement. White oak and red oak differ in color and ray density but share the characteristic ray structure. Oak is dense and durable; it was the dominant furniture wood in England and northern Europe through the seventeenth century.

Walnut presents an irregular, swirling grain with a color range from chocolate brown to gray-brown. The grain is open but not as prominent as oak. American black walnut was the dominant furniture wood in the American colonies and early republic. European walnut is similar but often slightly lighter and tighter in grain. Walnut was superseded by mahogany in fashionable American and English furniture from about 1730 onward.

Mahogany presents a straight, tight grain with a reddish-brown to warm brown base tone. Cuban mahogany (favored before 1830) is denser and darker than Honduran mahogany (dominant after 1830). The grain shows a subtle ribbon figure in quartersawn surfaces. Mahogany was the dominant wood of Georgian and Federal furniture in England and America. Its reddish tone deepens with age and exposure to light.

Cherry starts pale and ambiguous in color but deepens over decades of light exposure to a rich amber-red. Old cherry is often mistaken for mahogany by inexperienced buyers. The grain is fine and tight, with small pores and occasional small gum pockets or dark streaks. Cherry was favored by New England furniture makers and was common in New Hampshire, Vermont, and Connecticut work.

Maple is pale, nearly white, with very tight, dense grain. Curly maple and bird's-eye maple are figured variants with extraordinary visual appeal that commanded premium prices. Maple was used extensively in New England, where it was abundant. Painted furniture in New England was often maple or birch beneath the paint.

Pine varies by species. Eastern white pine is soft, pale, and wide-grained, with large soft-tissue areas between narrow grain lines. Southern yellow pine is harder and denser, with resin channels. Pine was the dominant secondary wood in American furniture (the wood used for drawer interiors, back panels, and other non-show surfaces in walnut or mahogany case pieces). A piece with a pine secondary wood and a mahogany primary wood is likely American rather than English.

Rosewood is dense, dark (deep brown to near-black), and often streaked with black. The grain is irregular and striking. Brazilian rosewood was used extensively in Victorian furniture and in Regency-era pieces. Indian rosewood is similar and was widely used in mid-century Danish and Scandinavian furniture.

Teak is oily, dense, and warm golden-brown to medium brown in color. It resists moisture and was used extensively in Scandinavian and Danish mid-century modern furniture (1945-1975). Its oily nature means it does not readily accept stains; most teak furniture shows its natural color. Teak in combination with tapered legs and simple joinery strongly indicates Danish or Scandinavian mid-century origin.

Satinwood is pale lemon-yellow with a fine, lustrous grain showing a satin sheen. It was used as a primary wood in Sheraton-style English furniture (1780-1810) and as a banding material in Federal American furniture.

Ebony is nearly black with minimal visible grain and a dense, heavy appearance. It was used as an accent material (stringing, inlay, turned elements) and in some East Asian furniture.

### Dating from Wood Character

Old-growth wood shows tight annual rings because trees grew slowly in dense forests. Plantation-growth wood (post-1930s) shows wider rings. Where end-grain is visible (an open drawer, an unfinished edge, a broken area), tight rings support an earlier date. Wide rings suggest more recent manufacture.

Shrinkage in panel construction indicates genuine age. A solid wood panel set into a frame will shrink across the grain over decades. A gap visible at one side of a frame-and-panel construction, or a crack developing at a previously glued edge, is evidence of real wood movement over real time. Reproductions assembled with modern adhesives and kiln-dried lumber may not show this movement.

## Metal Identification

### Non-Ferrous Metals

Brass appears warm yellow with a tone between gold and yellow. Aged brass develops a warm brown patina and, in recesses or damp conditions, green-blue verdigris. Cast brass is heavier than stamped brass. Look for seam lines along edges to indicate stamped (sheet metal) construction versus no seams in cast construction. Brass hardware on American furniture (bail pulls, escutcheons, cotter-pin-backed hardware) is a strong period indicator when the hardware shows period-appropriate casting and fitting methods.

Copper is distinctly reddish-orange and develops green verdigris in areas of moisture contact. Copper objects include kettles, measures, weathervanes, cooking vessels, and decorative arts. American copper weathervanes and tavern signs are a significant collecting category.

Sterling silver is 92.5% pure silver and appears bright white with a characteristic gray-black tarnish in recessed and unpolished areas. The weight of sterling is a tactile indicator (not available from photographs) but the appearance of tarnish pattern and the visual warmth of the metal surface help distinguish it from silver plate. Silver plate shows wear-through at points of contact (the high points on flatware, the base rim of hollow ware) where the base metal (usually nickel silver or Britannia metal) shows through as a yellowish or coppery tone.

Pewter is a tin-lead alloy (earlier formulations) or tin-antimony-copper alloy (modern "lead-free" pewter). It has a soft, muted gray appearance, lighter in visual tone than silver, and develops a gray oxide layer rather than the black tarnish of silver. Early American pewter (pre-1850) was often cast by itinerant pewterers and bears touchmarks (maker stamps) on the base.

Chrome is a mirror-bright silver, harder in appearance than pewter or nickel. Chromium plating was not commercially viable before 1924 and did not become common until the late 1920s. Any chrome-finished item is definitively post-1920. Chrome in combination with Bakelite, streamline styling, or glass indicates Art Deco manufacture (1925-1940).

Nickel plating is a warm silver-gray, less mirror-like than chrome. It was common before chrome became dominant. Nickel silver (German silver) is an alloy that looks silver but contains no silver; it was widely used for electroplated hollow ware.

## Ceramic Material Identification

Porcelain is vitrified (glass-like) at the body level. In thin sections, it transmits light. The base is smooth, hard, and resonant when struck (a clear ring tone). Chinese and Japanese hard-paste porcelain was the original; European factories developed their own formulations from the early eighteenth century.

Bone china is the whitest and most translucent of the porcelain family, incorporating bone ash. It is the dominant English tableware porcelain.

Stoneware is fired at high temperatures to near-vitrification but is opaque and dense. Salt-glazed stoneware has a characteristic "orange peel" surface texture. Stoneware jugs, crocks, and preserve jars are American redware and stoneware in the nineteenth-century domestic tradition.

Earthenware is porous and fired at lower temperatures. Unglazed earthenware absorbs water. The clay body is visible at the base; red earthenware clay bodies indicate traditional low-fire traditions. Majolica and other tin-glazed earthenware traditions fall in this category.

## Glass Material Identification

Pontil marks on the base of antique glass indicate hand-blowing. The pontil scar is a rough circular area where the glassblower's iron was broken away. Fire-polished pontil scars were smoothed in the heat; rough pontil scars were not. Mold-blown glass has seams; the position and character of those seams dates the piece.

Brilliant cut glass (American Brilliant period, 1880-1915) has deeply cut, sharp geometric patterns that create a prismatic sparkle. The glass itself is heavy lead crystal.

Depression glass (1929-1940) is lighter, often with a slight greenish or pinkish cast from the manganese or selenium used in manufacture, and shows mold seams through the pattern.

## Textile Material Identification

Silk has a natural luminosity and drapes with fluidity. It reflects light differently from synthetic satins. Pre-synthetic silk textiles were produced before the mid-twentieth century; post-1950 textiles may substitute rayon or polyester for silk.

Wool appears matte and has visible weight and texture. Wool's lanolin content gives it a slight sheen different from synthetic fibers. Worsted wool (a tightly woven, smooth-faced cloth) differs from woolen-spun cloth (softer, with a slight nap).

Linen has a characteristic slubbed texture from the irregular flax fiber bundles. It is cool to the touch and typically shows slight color irregularities in the weave. Linen was dominant before cotton became the primary fiber in the mid-nineteenth century.

## Period-Appropriate Material Combinations

Certain material combinations serve as strong period confirmation when all elements agree. Walnut with brass bail pulls and a pine secondary wood is consistent with American colonial or early federal furniture. Mahogany with satinwood inlay and a straight-grained oak secondary wood is consistent with English Hepplewhite or Sheraton work. Teak with Danish cord seating or woven seat material is consistent with mid-century Scandinavian design. Chrome with Bakelite handles and streamline form is consistent with Art Deco American domestic goods. Cast iron with a japanned black finish and decorative stenciling is consistent with Victorian American decorative cast iron (1840-1890).

When material combinations conflict with the proposed period or style, flag the conflict explicitly. A piece claimed as pre-Civil War furniture but built with Phillips-head screws, or a piece in the Chippendale style but using machine-cut dovetails and wide-ring pine, requires investigation and honest reporting of the discrepancy.

## Material Impact on Value Routing and Specialist Bot Triggering

Sterling silver: triggers price range escalation and hallmark examination protocol. Route to antique check if marks indicate pre-1975 manufacture.

Hand-blown glass with pontil mark: triggers antique check. Brilliant cut glass triggers antique check and collector interest assessment.

Old-growth tight-grain hardwood: triggers antique check. The wood itself is an age indicator.

Silk textile with hand embroidery: triggers antique check and textile specialist consideration.

Bone china or hard-paste porcelain with factory mark: triggers maker mark protocol and antique check if mark indicates pre-1975 manufacture.
