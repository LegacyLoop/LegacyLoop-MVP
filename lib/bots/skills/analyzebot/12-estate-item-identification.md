# Estate Item Identification

## The Estate Sale Context

The majority of items processed by LegacyLoop come from estates. The seller is often a son, daughter, grandchild, or executor who inherited the item along with a house full of other things and no documentation. They typically know three facts: it was in grandma's house, it was important to someone, and they need to sell it.

This is not a failure of information on the seller's part. Estate items routinely arrive at auction houses in exactly this condition. Expert identification from limited information is a core professional skill, not a special case.

AnalyzeBot's job in estate contexts is to extract maximum identification value from whatever evidence is available — the item itself, the photos, the background, and any description the seller provides — while being honest about what cannot be determined.

## Using Background Context Clues

Photos taken in the home often contain more identifying information than the item itself. Train your analysis to look at the full frame, not just the subject.

Style of the surrounding space: a Victorian parlor with ornate trim and heavy drapery suggests a collection period of 1870-1910. A ranch house with exposed brick and shag carpet suggests 1960s-1975. A craftsman bungalow with built-in bookcases and wainscoting suggests 1905-1930.

Other items visible alongside: items placed together in a home were usually acquired together or in the same era. If the subject item sits next to a clearly 1950s television set and a mid-century lamp, the collection date likely aligns. If it sits on a table with Depression-era glass and a crank telephone, the household was likely furnished in the 1920s-1940s.

Floor coverings and wall treatments: hooked rugs suggest rural New England pre-1960. Linoleum with a geometric pattern suggests 1940s-1960s. Avocado and harvest gold appliances visible in the background reliably place the household's primary furnishing period in 1968-1978.

Architectural details: six-over-six sash windows suggest pre-1900 construction. Picture rail molding at ceiling height suggests pre-1940. Aluminum windows suggest post-1950. These are the home's own date stamps.

## Generational Heuristics for New England Estates

Most LegacyLoop users are in Maine and the broader New England region. New England estate collections have predictable patterns by generation.

Items from households primarily furnished 1900-1930: Colonial Revival furniture (maple, birch, painted), pressed glass (Heisey, Cambridge, Indiana), granite ware and enamelware kitchen items, wicker furniture, Victorian silverplate flatware, tintype and cabinet card photographs, early electrified oil lamps.

Items from households primarily furnished 1930-1950: Depression glass (pink, green, amber — Anchor Hocking, Hazel-Atlas, Jeanette), Fiestaware (pre-1972 colors), bakelite radio and appliance handles, chrome dinette sets, Griswold cast iron, Art Deco decorative arts, wartime aluminum and tin items.

Items from households primarily furnished 1950-1970: early Pyrex (Primary Colors and Gooseberry patterns), Melmac dinnerware, vinyl upholstered furniture, teak and walnut Scandinavian-influenced pieces, early aluminum outdoor furniture, transistor radios, rotary-dial telephones, 45 rpm and 33 rpm record collections, early television sets.

Items from households primarily furnished 1970-1990: earthenware pottery, macrame, Harvest Gold and Avocado Green appliances and ceramics, oak veneer furniture, Corelle, early personal computers and electronics, designer-era collectibles (Franklin Mint, Bradford Exchange plates).

These heuristics are starting points, not conclusions. Many households acquired items across multiple decades. A 1970s household may still have furniture from 1890. Apply heuristics to help narrow the era, then look for confirming construction evidence.

## Squeezing Maximum Identification from Limited Photos

When the seller provides one or two photos and no description, work systematically through everything visible.

Labels and tags: look for paper labels on the back, bottom, or inside surfaces. Even partially visible text can identify a manufacturer. A paper label with a city name and a telephone number with fewer than seven digits (pre-1958 in most US markets) dates the item to before 1958.

Construction details at the edges of the frame: visible drawer joints (dovetail vs machine-cut), the species of secondary wood in a drawer interior, the type of fastener (cut nail vs wire nail vs screw). These are the diagnostic details that antiquarians use and they are often visible in casual photos if you look for them.

Back and underside markings: if a seller photographs the bottom of a ceramic piece or the back of a painting, these surfaces carry the most identifying information. Pottery marks, firing signatures, canvas stamps, frame maker's labels.

Hardware: pulls, hinges, locks, and casters on furniture are era-specific. Oval brass bail pulls = 18th century or revival. Pressed glass pulls = 1870-1900. Wooden knobs = Shaker or country style, period or revival. Bakelite or chrome hardware = 1930s-1950s.

Patina and aging: the way a surface ages is hard to fake convincingly. Genuine oxidation on silver, verdigris on copper, crazing on old glazes, shrinkage cracks in old wood panels — these are natural aging signatures that help confirm era.

## Handling Items the Seller Cannot Identify

Sellers sometimes photograph items they cannot name at all — a device whose function is unknown, a tool whose use is forgotten, a decorative object with no obvious purpose.

When you encounter an unidentified functional object: describe its most likely function based on form, then name the specific features that support that conclusion. "This appears to be a whale oil lamp based on the double-wick burner and the tall cylindrical font, consistent with the pre-kerosene era (pre-1860 in New England)."

When you encounter an unidentified decorative object: describe the decorative vocabulary (the symbols, scenes, or motifs depicted), then name the tradition or period most likely to have produced it. "The painted scene shows a rural winter landscape with a horse-drawn sleigh, consistent with 19th-century American decorative painting."

When you genuinely cannot identify the object's category: say so, but describe it precisely. Every observation you make — material, method of manufacture, visible wear patterns, any markings — is useful information even when you cannot name the category.

## Maintaining Seller Dignity

Many sellers feel embarrassed that they do not know what they are selling. The platform experience should never reinforce that embarrassment.

Framing that maintains dignity: "This is an interesting piece — the construction details suggest it predates 1900. I would love to see a photo of the back to help me confirm the maker."

Framing that undermines dignity: "I cannot tell what this is from these photos."

The seller's uncertainty is not a failure. It is the reason the platform exists. Every "I don't know what grandma had" is an opportunity for the platform to add value by knowing what grandma had.

When requesting additional photos, be specific about what you need and why. "A photo of the bottom of the piece in natural light would help me read the maker's mark" is actionable. "Better photos" is not.

## How Estate Context Adds Provenance Value

Provenance — the documented history of an item's ownership — is a genuine value factor in antiques and art markets. Estate context is informal provenance, and it matters even without documentation.

"From a single-family estate in coastal Maine, continuously occupied since 1920" is a provenance statement. It tells a buyer that the item was not repainted by a dealer, was not stored in a facility, and reflects the collecting taste of a specific household over a specific period.

When the seller provides estate context (even casual mentions like "this was in my grandmother's house in Rockland"), capture this in the summary field. It becomes part of the listing and can support a higher price, particularly for items where authenticity is a buyer concern.

Provenance weight is strongest for: paintings and works on art, silver and jewelry, first-edition books, documented collections (a seller who says "grandpa collected these for 40 years"), and items with a regional identity (Maine-made, New Hampshire-crafted).
